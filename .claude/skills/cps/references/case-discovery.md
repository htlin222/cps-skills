# Case Discovery Guide

## Search Strategies by Source

### NEJM Clinical Problem Solving
- **Query**: `"clinical problem-solving" site:nejm.org [topic]`
- **Format**: Sequential reveal of case data with expert reasoning at each step
- **Best for**: Complex diagnostic reasoning, multi-step cases

### NEJM Case Records of Massachusetts General Hospital
- **Query**: `"case records" site:nejm.org [topic]`
- **Format**: Full case presentation followed by differential discussion and final diagnosis
- **Best for**: Classic teaching cases, pathology correlation

### BMJ Case Reports
- **Query**: `"case report" site:casereports.bmj.com [topic]`
- **Format**: Structured case report with discussion
- **Best for**: Unusual presentations, rare diseases

### JAMA Clinical Challenge
- **Query**: `"clinical challenge" site:jamanetwork.com [topic]`
- **Format**: Brief case with multiple-choice diagnostic question
- **Best for**: Focused diagnostic puzzles, image-based diagnosis

### Annals of Internal Medicine
- **Query**: `"clinical decisions" OR "clinical challenge" site:annals.org [topic]`
- **Format**: Case-based clinical decision-making exercises
- **Best for**: Management dilemmas, evidence-based decision cases

### Additional Sources
- Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR): Infectious and public health cases
- Hospital Medicine cases: `"clinical care conundrum" site:journalofhospitalmedicine.com`
- Radiology case collections: Radiopaedia.org, RadiologyAssistant.nl

## Formatting a Discovered Case into SCENARIO.md

Extract and organize the following elements from the source case:

1. **Demographics**: Age, sex, and clinical setting (ED, outpatient, ICU)
2. **Chief complaint**: In concise clinical language
3. **HPI**: Chronological narrative preserving pertinent positives and negatives
4. **PMH / Medications / Allergies**: Itemized lists
5. **Social and Family History**: Include tobacco, alcohol, drugs, occupation, travel, sexual history
6. **Vital signs**: T, HR, BP, RR, SpO2 (with O2 delivery method)
7. **Physical exam**: Organized by system, preserving key positive and negative findings
8. **Labs**: Organized by panel (CBC, CMP, UA, coags, special studies)
9. **Imaging**: Describe findings as reported; do not include the final diagnosis interpretation
10. **Additional studies**: ECG, PFTs, cultures, biopsies

**Key rule**: Remove any mention of the final diagnosis from the scenario. The case should present data without revealing the answer.

## Difficulty Grading

| Level | Presentation | Diagnosis | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Classic | Common | Textbook presentation of a common disease | Crushing chest pain with ST elevation = STEMI |
| 2 | Atypical | Common | Unusual features of a common disease | Diabetic patient with painless MI presenting as dyspnea |
| 3 | Classic | Uncommon | Textbook presentation of a rare disease | Malar rash + arthritis + renal failure = SLE |
| 4 | Atypical | Uncommon | Unusual features of a rare disease | Pheochromocytoma presenting as cyclic vomiting |
| 5 | Mimics / Complex | Any | Red herrings, concurrent diagnoses, or great mimickers | Sarcoidosis mimicking lymphoma with concurrent TB |

## Challenge Case Criteria

A good CPS challenge case should have:

1. **Sufficient data**: Enough history, exam, labs, and imaging to reason through (not just a one-liner)
2. **Diagnostic tension**: At least 3-4 plausible diagnoses in the initial differential
3. **Pivotal findings**: Specific data points that significantly shift the probability (high LR findings)
4. **Must-not-miss inclusion**: At least one dangerous diagnosis that must be actively excluded
5. **Teaching value**: Illustrates an important clinical principle, common pitfall, or reasoning strategy
6. **Bayesian applicability**: Findings with known or estimable LRs to enable quantitative updating
7. **Resolvability**: The case should have a definitive or strongly supported final diagnosis

**Avoid**: Cases with insufficient data, purely management-focused cases (no diagnostic uncertainty), or cases requiring subspecialty knowledge not covered by the CPS personas.
