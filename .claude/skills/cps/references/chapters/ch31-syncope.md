## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Two pivotal steps: (1) Distinguish syncope from nonsyncopal causes of transient LOC (seizure, hypoglycemia, CVA). Syncope = abrupt onset + brief duration + spontaneous complete recovery. (2) Classify syncope as reflex, orthostatic, or cardiac. Cardiac syncope carries markedly increased mortality and risk of sudden death.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Vasovagal (neurocardiogenic) | 20-33% of syncope | Trigger (standing, pain, emotion), prodrome (nausea, warmth) | No |
| Cardiac arrhythmia (VT, bradycardia) | ~15% | Supine/sitting/exertion, no prodrome, cardiac history | Yes |
| Orthostatic hypotension | ~10% | Immediately upon standing, dehydration, medications | No |
| Aortic stenosis | Uncommon | Exertional syncope, systolic murmur, elderly | Yes |
| HCM | 0.02-0.23% | Exertional syncope, young athletes, family hx sudden death | Yes |
| PE | Uncommon | Dyspnea, pleuritic pain, RV dysfunction | Yes |
| Long QT syndrome | Rare | Family hx sudden death, emotional triggers, prolonged QTc | Yes |
| Situational syncope | Common | With cough, micturition, or defecation | No |
| Carotid sinus syndrome | Uncommon | Head turning, tight collar, elderly | No |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. **Is this syncope?** Abrupt onset + brief + spontaneous recovery = syncope. Prolonged confusion (>1-2 min) = nonsyncopal (seizure?)
2. **ALL syncopal patients**: Thorough history, physical exam, ECG
3. **Classify syncope type**:
   - **Cardiac clues**: Prior cardiac disease, supine/sitting/exertional syncope, chest pain, palpitations, dyspnea, family hx sudden death, age >60, abnormal ECG, murmur, JVD, S3, edema
   - **Orthostatic clues**: Syncope immediately on standing, orthostatic BP changes
   - **Reflex clues**: Trigger + prodrome (nausea, warmth, sweating)
4. **Suspected cardiac syncope**: ADMIT; echo (significant abnormalities in 27%), cardiac monitoring
5. **If neither orthostatic nor reflex fits**: suspect cardiac by exclusion
6. **ECG clues to specific causes**: Prior MI/long QT = VT; bradycardia/AV block = conduction disease; BBB = AV block or VT; delta wave = WPW; S1Q3T3 = PE
7. **ILR**: Diagnostic yield 37-55% in recurrent unexplained syncope; 19% have severe bradyarrhythmias
8. **EPS**: Sens 90% for VT; yield 50% with heart disease vs 10% without

## Clinical Pearls
- Syncope DURING exercise = cardiac; syncope AFTER exercise = vasovagal (venous pooling)
- 30% of dilated cardiomyopathy deaths are sudden cardiac death
- HCM: most common cause of cardiovascular death in young athletes
- Long QT syndrome: arrhythmias can be triggered by emotional stress
- Tilt-table testing: limited accuracy (26-56% sensitive); positive results NOT specific for vasovagal
- ILRs identified alternative arrhythmia in 5-16% of patients with presumed vasovagal syncope
- Counterpressure maneuvers (arm tensing, squatting): reduce vasovagal syncope (NNT 5)
- Reducing BP medications in recurrent vasovagal: NNT 3
- Pacemakers for select severe cardioinhibitory reflex syncope: NNT 3
- Atrial fibrillation in HCM: OR 17.7 for thromboembolism vs sinus rhythm
- Clinical judgment more sensitive for serious 30-day events than scoring systems (94% vs ~75%)
- Preparticipation screening with ECG reduces sudden cardiac death in athletes by 79% (mainly from HCM detection)
- BBB + syncope: AV block develops in 17%; pacemaker prevents syncope but not sudden death

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Syncope during exertion: aortic stenosis, HCM, arrhythmia
- Syncope while supine or sitting: cardiac cause until proven otherwise
- Family history of sudden death + syncope: HCM, long QT, Brugada
- New murmur + syncope: aortic stenosis, HCM
- Abnormal ECG (BBB, long QT, preexcitation, ischemia): cardiac workup mandatory
- Prior MI or HF + syncope: very high risk for VT -- ADMIT
- Prolonged confusion after event (>1-2 min): likely NOT syncope -- evaluate for seizure
