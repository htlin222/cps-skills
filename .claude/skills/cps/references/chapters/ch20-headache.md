# Ch20: Headache

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Primary pivotal point: Is the headache NEW or OLD? Old headaches are usually primary (benign); new headaches are more likely secondary (potentially dangerous). Secondary pivotal points: severity, quality change, associated neurologic symptoms, age of onset. Primary headaches diagnosed clinically (IHS criteria); secondary headaches diagnosed by identifying underlying disease.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Tension headache | Most common (63% M, 86% F) | Bilateral, pressing/squeezing, mild-moderate, not worsened by activity | No |
| Migraine | 90% of initial headache visits | Unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, nausea, photo/phonophobia, disabling | No |
| Cluster headache | 0.1% prevalence (M>F 4:1) | Severe periorbital, unilateral, autonomic symptoms, 15-180 min, clusters | No |
| SAH | 1-4% of ED headaches | Thunderclap, "worst headache of life," neck stiffness, altered mental status | Yes |
| Brain tumor | Rare without known cancer | Morning headaches, focal deficits, seizures; isolated headache in only 2% | Yes |
| Temporal arteritis | Age >50 | New headache, jaw claudication, visual symptoms, elevated ESR/CRP | Yes |
| Medication overuse headache | Common | Chronic daily headache, >15 days/month, ergotamine/triptan/NSAID/opioid overuse | No |
| Carbon monoxide poisoning | Uncommon | Morning/location-specific headache, multiple affected people, winter | Yes |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Old headache -> likely primary -> classify: migraine (POUNDing), tension (bilateral, pressing), cluster (autonomic)
2. New headache in patient >50 -> consider temporal arteritis, brain tumor, medication overuse
3. Thunderclap headache -> assume SAH until proven otherwise -> non-contrast head CT immediately
4. If CT negative for SAH -> lumbar puncture (delay 6-12h for xanthochromia sensitivity if safe)
5. Morning headaches -> consider brain tumor, OSA, nocturnal hypoglycemia, caffeine withdrawal, CO poisoning
6. Neuroimaging indications: abnormal neuro exam, atypical aura, increasing frequency, awakens from sleep, age >50, first/worst/thunderclap, cancer/immunosuppressed, exertional trigger

## Clinical Pearls
- Severity is less important than quality change; a mild headache dissimilar to prior headaches is more worrisome than a severe one identical to chronic headaches
- 50% of migraineurs have nonpulsatile headaches; 40% have bilateral headaches
- Brain tumors almost never present as isolated headache without known cancer (only 2%)
- 25% of SAH patients are initially misdiagnosed; misdiagnosed patients are half as likely to have good outcomes
- SAH mortality is ~50%
- Caffeine withdrawal: suspect when headaches occur on weekends/vacations or when sleeping late
- Sentinel headaches may precede SAH by weeks (controversial but important concept)
- Exertional headaches are clinically indistinguishable from SAH -- must evaluate to rule out SAH
- CO poisoning: suspect if headache occurs only in one location, multiple people affected, winter season

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Thunderclap headache ("worst headache of life") -> SAH until proven otherwise -> CT then LP
- New headache + focal neurologic deficits -> imaging for tumor, hemorrhage, or mass
- New headache age >50 + jaw claudication + visual symptoms -> temporal arteritis -> ESR + temporal artery biopsy
- Headache + fever + neck stiffness -> meningitis -> LP
- New headache in cancer/immunosuppressed patient -> imaging for metastasis/infection
- Headache + papilledema -> raised intracranial pressure -> urgent imaging
- Headache triggered by Valsalva/cough lasting >30 min -> Chiari malformation workup
