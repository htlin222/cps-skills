# Ch19: GI Bleeding

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Anatomic framework: upper (proximal to ligament of Treitz) vs lower (distal, primarily colonic). Pivotal points: hematemesis/coffee grounds (upper), melena (usually upper), hematochezia (usually lower but 10-15% upper). First priority is hemodynamic stabilization before diagnosis. Risk stratification with Glasgow-Blatchford Score (upper) or clinical predictors (lower).

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Peptic ulcer disease | 50% of upper GI bleeds | Dyspepsia, NSAID/aspirin use, H. pylori, melena/hematemesis | Yes |
| Esophageal varices | 2nd most common upper | Cirrhosis, alcohol, hematemesis, worst prognosis of all GI bleeds | Yes |
| Mallory-Weiss tear | Common upper | Hematemesis after vomiting (but vomiting precedes bleeding in only 1/3) | No |
| Diverticular bleed | 35% of lower GI bleeds | Painless bright red blood per rectum, older adults, usually self-limited | No |
| Colonic malignancy/polyp | 7% of lower | Weight loss, change in bowel habits, iron deficiency anemia | Yes |
| Colitis (IBD, ischemic, infectious) | 14% of lower | Abdominal pain, diarrhea, bloody stools | Yes |
| Angiodysplasia | 3% of lower | Older adults, right colon, usually occult or slow bleed, ESRD association | No |
| Hemorrhoids/fissures | 12% of anorectal | Small volume bright red blood, pain (fissures), rarely hemodynamically significant | No |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Stabilize: 2 large-bore IVs (<=16 gauge), type and cross 2 units PRBCs, assess volume status
2. Risk stratify: Glasgow-Blatchford (upper); clinical predictors (lower)
3. Determine upper vs lower: hematemesis/coffee grounds -> upper; bright red per rectum -> usually lower (but 10-15% upper)
4. If upper suspected -> start IV PPI +/- octreotide; EGD within 12-24 hours
5. If variceal bleed suspected -> octreotide + antibiotics (ceftriaxone/norfloxacin) before endoscopy
6. If lower suspected and stable -> colonoscopy after prep
7. If persistent bleeding with negative endoscopy -> radionuclide scintigraphy or angiography
8. Transfusion: Hb <9 if actively bleeding; Hb <7 if not bleeding; Hb <8 if cardiovascular disease

## Clinical Pearls
- Even after large hemorrhage, Hb may initially be normal -- falls only after fluid resuscitation
- 10-15% of patients with hematochezia have an upper GI source (especially if hemodynamically unstable)
- Blood is a cathartic: brisk upper GI bleed can present as bright red blood per rectum
- Mallory-Weiss: vomiting precedes bleeding in only about 1/3 of cases
- Variceal bleeds: 33% mortality at first hemorrhage; 60% rebleed within 1 year; worst prognosis of all GI bleeds
- Diverticular bleed: 75% stop spontaneously; 40% recur; right-sided diverticula bleed more often
- Large-bore peripheral IVs much more effective than central lines for volume resuscitation (flow proportional to r^4)
- 50% of patients with bleeding peptic ulcers have had no previous symptoms

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Hematemesis + cirrhosis stigmata -> variceal bleed -> octreotide + antibiotics + urgent EGD
- Orthostasis + tachycardia -> 20-25% volume depletion -> aggressive resuscitation
- Signs of shock -> 30-40% volume depletion -> immediate transfusion
- Active bleeding + hemodynamic instability -> 3x risk of poor outcomes
- Any upper GI bleed in a patient with liver disease -> assume variceal until proven otherwise
- Hepatic venous pressure gradient >20 mmHg predicts poor outcomes in variceal bleeding
