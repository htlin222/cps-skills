# Ch2: Screening and Health Maintenance

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Screening Principles
- Screen only when: disease is serious, prevalence adequate, test accurate, early treatment improves outcomes
- Higher yield in high-risk groups (prevalence affects PPV)
- Avoid lead time bias (earlier detection without survival benefit) and length time bias (screening preferentially detects slow-growing disease)
- Best outcome measure: disease-specific or all-cause mortality in randomized trials

## USPSTF Grading System
| Grade | Recommendation | Action |
|-------|---------------|--------|
| A | High certainty of substantial net benefit | Offer/provide |
| B | High certainty moderate OR moderate certainty substantial benefit | Offer/provide |
| C | Moderate certainty of small benefit | Offer selectively |
| D | No benefit or harms outweigh benefit | Discourage |
| I | Insufficient evidence | Clinical judgment |

## Cholesterol & CVD Risk
- Per 38 mg/dL rise in LDL above 118: RR 1.42 (men), 1.37 (women)
- Per 15.5 mg/dL rise in HDL: RR 0.64 (men), 0.69 (women)
- Statin primary prevention: all-cause mortality RRR 14% (NNT 138/5y); CVD events RRR 25% (NNT 49/5y)

## Clinical Pearls
- PPV depends on prevalence: same test performs differently in high-risk vs low-risk populations
- Single FOBT after DRE (9% sensitivity) should NEVER be used for CRC screening
- PSA screening: shared decision-making (men 55-69); harms include overdiagnosis/overtreatment
- Mammography sensitivity lower in younger women and dense breasts (LR+ drops from 22 to 6)
- Low-dose CT lung screening: 96% of positive results are false-positives
- 93-100% of squamous cervical cancers contain high-risk HPV DNA
- Cumulative false-positive mammogram risk: 50-60% over 10 years
