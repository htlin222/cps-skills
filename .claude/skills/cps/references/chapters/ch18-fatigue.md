# Ch18: Fatigue

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Organ/system approach. Distinguish fatigue from weakness, excessive sleepiness, and dyspnea on exertion. Psychiatric and sleep disorders account for most cases. A specific medical disease (other than psych/sleep) is found in <10% of primary care fatigue patients. Up to 75% of fatigued patients have psychiatric symptoms; 80% in referral clinics have sleep disorders.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Depression | Very common (up to 75%) | Anhedonia, weight change, sleep disturbance, somatic symptoms | No |
| Anxiety (GAD, panic, social) | Very common | Excessive worry, restlessness, muscle tension, irritability | No |
| Insomnia | Very common (35-50% adults) | Difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep, daytime impairment | No |
| Obstructive sleep apnea | Common (15% men, 5% women) | Snoring, obesity, morning headaches, daytime sleepiness | No |
| Hypothyroidism | Must-not-miss | Weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin | Yes |
| Anemia | Must-not-miss | Pallor, tachycardia, dyspnea on exertion | Yes |
| Diabetes mellitus | Must-not-miss | Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss | Yes |
| Chronic fatigue syndrome | Diagnosis of exclusion | >6 months fatigue, post-exertional malaise, unrefreshing sleep | No |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Clarify symptom: fatigue vs weakness vs sleepiness vs dyspnea
2. Screen for psychiatric disorders: PHQ-9 (depression), GAD-2/GAD-7 (anxiety)
3. Detailed sleep history: sleep hygiene, snoring, apneas, schedule, substance use
4. Basic labs for all patients: CBC, BMP (glucose, electrolytes, BUN, Cr, calcium, LFTs), TSH
5. If sleep disorder suspected -> polysomnography (gold standard for OSA)
6. If psychiatric + sleep causes excluded and labs normal -> consider chronic fatigue syndrome, autoimmune, chronic infection
7. OSA risk factors: obesity (strongest), male sex, postmenopausal, neck circumference >16" women / >17" men

## Clinical Pearls
- Always ask patients what they mean by "fatigue" -- may actually be weakness, sleepiness, or dyspnea
- Multiple somatic complaints strongly suggest psychiatric etiology
- Patients who feel tired "constantly" are more likely to have a psychiatric cause
- 6-fold increase in OSA risk with 10% weight gain
- 50% of OSA patients have hypertension; majority of resistant HTN patients have OSA
- OSA consequences: motor vehicle accidents (RR 2.5-5), HTN (RR 2.89), HF (RR 2.38)
- CBT for insomnia has similar short-term efficacy to medications but better sustained long-term
- Depressive symptoms occur in up to 50% of OSA patients -- treat OSA before diagnosing depression

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Fatigue + weight loss + lymphadenopathy -> evaluate for malignancy
- Fatigue + cold intolerance + goiter -> check TSH for hypothyroidism
- Fatigue + pallor + tachycardia -> CBC for anemia
- Fatigue + polyuria + polydipsia -> check glucose for diabetes
- Fatigue + snoring + observed apneas + morning headaches + obesity -> polysomnography for OSA
