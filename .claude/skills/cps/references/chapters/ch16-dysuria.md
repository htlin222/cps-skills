# Ch16: Dysuria

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Anatomic approach: skin (herpes, contact dermatitis) -> urethra (STI urethritis) -> male genital (prostatitis, epididymitis) -> female genital (vaginitis, cervicitis) -> bladder (cystitis) -> kidney (pyelonephritis). Pivotal points: vaginal/penile discharge, flank pain, fever, nausea/vomiting, rectal/perineal pain, urinary hesitancy. Pelvic exam in women with discharge; prostate exam in men with cystitis symptoms.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Uncomplicated cystitis | Most common cause | Dysuria, frequency, urgency; no discharge/fever | No |
| Complicated cystitis | Common | Same + male, pregnant, catheter, obstruction, immunosuppressed | No |
| Vaginitis (bacterial vaginosis, candida, trichomonas) | Common in women | Vaginal discharge, odor, irritation, dyspareunia | No |
| Pyelonephritis | Less common | Flank/back pain, fever, chills, nausea/vomiting, CVA tenderness | Yes |
| Acute prostatitis | Males | Perineal/low back pain, fever, tender prostate, hesitancy | Yes |
| Urethritis (gonorrhea, chlamydia) | STI risk | Penile/cervical discharge, dysuria, dyspareunia | No |
| Urosepsis | Uncommon | Fever, hypotension, lethargy, SIRS criteria | Yes |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Dysuria in a woman without discharge or fever -> likely uncomplicated cystitis -> treat empirically, no testing needed
2. Vaginal discharge present -> pelvic exam, wet mount, pH, whiff test, KOH prep, trichomoniasis NAAT
3. Flank pain, fever, nausea/vomiting, CVA tenderness -> suspect pyelonephritis -> urine culture, consider imaging
4. Male with perineal pain, hesitancy, tender prostate -> acute prostatitis -> avoid vigorous DRE; urine culture + STI PCR
5. Penile/cervical discharge -> urethritis/cervicitis -> PCR for GC/CT; treat empirically
6. Systemic toxicity (SIRS criteria met) -> urosepsis -> hospitalize, IV antibiotics, fluids

## Clinical Pearls
- Women with dysuria + frequency and no vaginal symptoms have 90% probability of cystitis
- Negative LE and nitrite does NOT rule out cystitis (LR- only 0.3)
- 30-50% of women with cystitis have colony counts of only 10^2 to 10^4
- Symptomatic premenopausal women should be treated despite negative urine cultures
- Elderly patients may present with delirium or functional decline rather than classic UTI symptoms
- Vigorous DRE is contraindicated in suspected acute prostatitis (may worsen infection)
- Nitrofurantoin does NOT penetrate the prostate; ineffective for prostatitis
- Factors NOT associated with cystitis: wiping patterns, underwear type, postcoital voiding, douching, BMI

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Fever + flank pain + dysuria -> pyelonephritis (urine culture positive in 90%)
- SIRS criteria (WBC >12k or <4k, temp >38 or <36, HR >90, RR >20) + GU infection -> urosepsis -> ICU admission
- Male dysuria with perineal pain and systemic symptoms -> acute prostatitis -> prolonged antibiotics (4-6 weeks)
- Failure to improve in 48-72h on appropriate antibiotics -> imaging for abscess, obstruction, stone
