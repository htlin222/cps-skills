## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Organize by primary vs secondary; secondary causes use organ/system framework. 90-95% of hypertension is essential (primary). Pivotal approach: (1) assess target organ damage, (2) assess cardiovascular risk factors, (3) identify secondary causes only when clinical clues present or BP is resistant.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Essential (primary) HTN | 90-95% | Family history, age 20-50, gradual onset, no specific symptoms | No |
| Primary hyperaldosteronism | 5-10% (20% in resistant HTN) | Hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis, adrenal adenoma or hyperplasia | Yes |
| Renovascular (atherosclerotic) | 0.18-4.4% (higher in resistant HTN) | Abrupt onset or worsening HTN, vascular disease, renal bruit | Yes |
| Pheochromocytoma | 0.04-0.2% | Episodic HTN, headache, palpitations, diaphoresis (triad) | Yes |
| Cushing syndrome | 0.3% | Central obesity, striae, moon facies, proximal weakness | No |
| CKD | Common | Elevated creatinine, proteinuria | No |
| Sleep apnea | 60-70% of resistant HTN | Obesity, daytime sleepiness, snoring, morning headaches | No |
| Drug-induced | Common | NSAIDs, OCPs, cocaine, sympathomimetics, corticosteroids, cyclosporine | No |
| Fibromuscular dysplasia | Rare | Young women, renal artery stenosis | Yes |
| Coarctation of aorta | Rare | Upper extremity HTN, diminished femoral pulses, young | Yes |

## BP Classification (ACC/AHA 2017)
- **Normal**: SBP <120 AND DBP <80
- **Elevated**: SBP 120-129 AND DBP <80
- **Stage 1 HTN**: SBP 130-139 OR DBP 80-89
- **Stage 2 HTN**: SBP >=140 OR DBP >=90
- Prevalence of HTN with this definition: ~45% of US adults

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. **Confirm HTN**: Proper measurement technique (correct cuff size, seated, arm at heart level)
2. **Initial evaluation**: ECG, electrolytes, BUN/Cr, Ca, TSH, urine albumin-Cr ratio, fasting glucose, lipids
3. **Assess TOD**: LVH (ECG, echo), retinopathy, nephropathy (albumin-Cr ratio), vascular disease
4. **Assess CV risk**: 10-year ASCVD risk (Pooled Cohort Equations)
5. **Treatment initiation**:
   - Low risk (stage 1, ASCVD <10%, no TOD): lifestyle x 3-6 months
   - Moderate/high risk (stage 1 + ASCVD >=10%, or stage 2): lifestyle + medication simultaneously
6. **Screen for secondary causes** if: age <30 or >55 at onset, resistant HTN (uncontrolled on 3 drugs incl diuretic), clinical clues
7. **Resistant HTN workup**: Confirm adherence, exclude pseudoresistance (cuff size, white coat), then evaluate hyperaldosteronism, renovascular disease, sleep apnea, CKD

## Lifestyle Modifications for BP Reduction
- Weight loss: ~5 mm Hg per 5 kg lost
- DASH diet: 8-14 mm Hg reduction
- Sodium restriction (<1500 mg/day): 5-6 mm Hg
- Exercise (30 min/day): 4-9 mm Hg
- Alcohol moderation (<=2 drinks/day men, <=1 women): 2-4 mm Hg

## Clinical Pearls
- BP cuff too small can elevate systolic BP by up to 15 mm Hg
- Each 20/10 mm Hg increment above 115/75 doubles CVD risk
- Normotensive at age 55: >90% lifetime risk of developing HTN
- For every 10% increase in body weight, systolic BP increases by 6.5 mm Hg
- Alcohol causes HTN in ~10% of US hypertensive adults
- Family history absent in renovascular hypertension (unlike essential HTN)
- Hypokalemia with metabolic alkalosis: hallmark of aldosterone excess
- ACE inhibitors and ARBs should NOT be combined (accelerates CKD)
- ARAS present in >30% of patients undergoing cardiac catheterization
- ~50% of patients with renal artery stenosis have renovascular hypertension
- Flash pulmonary edema with normal systolic function: bilateral RAS
- Salt sensitivity increased in elderly, Black patients, and CKD

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Sudden onset severe HTN or refractory to 3 drugs: secondary cause
- Flash pulmonary edema with normal EF: bilateral renal artery stenosis
- Hypokalemia without diuretic use: primary hyperaldosteronism
- Episodic HTN + headache + palpitations + diaphoresis: pheochromocytoma
- New azotemia on ACE inhibitor/ARB: renovascular disease
- Asymmetric kidney size (>1.5 cm): renal artery stenosis
- BP difference between arms: coarctation or subclavian stenosis
- Diminished femoral pulses in young patient: coarctation of aorta
