# Ch30: Sore Throat

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Primary goal: identify and treat GABHS pharyngitis (prevent suppurative and nonsuppurative complications). Secondary: diagnose less common bacterial infections (Fusobacterium, gonorrhea) and important viral causes (primary HIV, influenza, mononucleosis). Pivotal points: exudate, unilateral symptoms (abscess), STI/HIV risk factors, signs of mono or influenza.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Viral URI (rhinovirus, coronavirus) | >=25% | Cough, coryza, rhinorrhea, hoarseness | No |
| GABHS pharyngitis | 5-15% adults, 20-30% children | Fever, exudate, anterior cervical LAD, no cough | Yes |
| Infectious mononucleosis (EBV) | ~2% adults | Fever + sore throat + LAD triad, age 15-24, fatigue | No |
| Influenza | Seasonal | Fever, myalgia, cough, acute onset | No |
| Fusobacterium necrophorum | Uncommon | Unilateral symptoms, Lemierre syndrome | Yes |
| Primary HIV | Rare | Pharyngitis, fever, rash, mucocutaneous ulcers, LAD | Yes |
| Peritonsillar abscess | Uncommon | Unilateral swelling, trismus, uvula deviation, muffled voice | Yes |
| Retropharyngeal abscess | Rare | Neck stiffness, dysphagia, drooling, airway compromise | Yes |

## Modified Centor Score Components
- +1: Tonsillar exudates
- +1: Swollen, tender anterior cervical nodes
- +1: Absence of cough
- +1: History of fever
- +1: Age <15 years
- -1: Age >45 years

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. **Calculate modified Centor score**
2. **Score 0-1**: No testing, symptomatic treatment (probability too low)
3. **Score 2-3**: RADT or throat culture; treat only if positive
4. **Score 4**: RADT or culture; some guidelines support empiric treatment
5. **Consider mononucleosis if**: age 15-24, fatigue, posterior cervical LAD, splenomegaly
6. **Consider primary HIV if**: high-risk behavior, mucocutaneous ulcers, rash + pharyngitis; send 4th-gen immunoassay AND viral load
7. **Consider abscess if**: unilateral swelling, trismus, uvula deviation, muffled voice, drooling
8. **No improvement at 48h on antibiotics**: reevaluate for abscess, wrong diagnosis, or resistant organism

## Clinical Pearls
- Viral pharyngitis: cough, coryza, rhinorrhea present; GABHS: absent
- Pretest probability of GABHS in adult clinic population: 5-10%
- Negative RADT in adults: no backup throat culture needed (low rheumatic fever risk)
- In children/adolescents: negative RADT must be confirmed with throat culture
- Untreated GABHS lasts 8-10 days; infectious during acute illness and 1 week after
- Antibiotics reduce rheumatic fever risk by 73%
- Clinical improvement expected within 48h; if not, reevaluate
- First-line GABHS treatment: penicillin or amoxicillin (narrow spectrum, low cost)
- Mononucleosis: 27-69% develop rash if given amoxicillin/ampicillin
- Splenomegaly in mono may persist; avoid contact sports for 4 weeks
- Splenic rupture: rare (1-2/1000) but potentially fatal
- Acute rheumatic fever: 1/1,000,000/year in US; still common in developing nations
- Peritonsillar abscess: most common deep head/neck infection (~30% of head/neck abscesses)
- Lemierre syndrome: F necrophorum causing internal jugular thrombosis + septic emboli

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Unilateral tonsillar swelling + trismus + uvula deviation: peritonsillar abscess (drain)
- Sore throat + high-risk behavior + rash + mucocutaneous ulcers: primary HIV
- No improvement at 48h on antibiotics: reevaluate (abscess? wrong diagnosis?)
- Sore throat + internal jugular thrombosis + septic emboli: Lemierre syndrome
- Drooling + stridor + high fever: consider epiglottitis (rare in adults)
- Tonsillectomy only indicated for >=4 severe episodes per year
