# Ch3: Abdominal Pain

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Organize by 4 pivotal points:
1. **Location** of pain (RUQ, epigastric, periumbilical, RLQ, LLQ, diffuse)
2. **Time course**: first acute episode vs recurrent acute vs chronic/subacute
3. **Peritoneal findings**: rebound, rigidity, guarding → intra-abdominal catastrophe
4. **Unexplained hypotension**: suggests sepsis, retroperitoneal hemorrhage, ruptured AAA
5. **Abdominal distention**: excess air (obstruction, perforation) vs fluid (ascites, hemorrhage)

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Appendicitis | 7% lifetime | RLQ pain migration, anorexia | Perforation risk increases with age |
| Biliary colic | 8% >40y, 20% >60y | Episodic RUQ/epigastric, post-prandial, <4h | Cholecystitis if >4-6h + fever |
| Ascending cholangitis | 5-20% of symptomatic stones | Charcot triad: jaundice 79%, fever 77%, RUQ pain 68% | Bacteremia in 74%; life-threatening |
| Acute pancreatitis | — | Epigastric boring pain radiating to back | 4% mortality; 20% develop severe disease |
| Bowel obstruction (SBO) | 76% of obstructions | Prior surgery, crampy pain, no flatus | Strangulation: 20-40% of complete SBO |
| Bowel obstruction (LBO) | 24% of obstructions | Cancer 53%, volvulus 17% | Infarction if untreated |
| AAA rupture | 5.6% lifetime risk | Triad: pain, pulsatile mass, hypotension (only 50%) | 81% mortality with rupture |
| Acute mesenteric ischemia | 1% of abdominal pain | Pain out of proportion to exam; atrial fibrillation | Mortality increases with delay |
| Ischemic colitis | Most common intestinal ischemia | Left-sided pain, hematochezia | Colonic infarction 15-20% |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Determine pain **location** to narrow DDx
2. Determine **time course**: first acute vs recurrent vs chronic
3. Assess for **peritoneal signs** → if present, consider AAA, perforation, ischemia, pancreatitis
4. Assess for **unexplained hypotension** → hemorrhage vs sepsis
5. Assess for **abdominal distention** → obstruction vs free fluid
6. For suspected appendicitis: CT is test of choice (LR+ 24, LR- 0.08)
7. For biliary disease: RUQ ultrasound → MRCP/EUS → ERCP based on risk stratification
8. For bowel obstruction: plain radiograph → CT scan → surgical consultation

## Clinical Pearls
- Diffuse pain that localizes and becomes constant suggests parietal peritoneal inflammation
- Distract the patient during palpation for better assessment of tenderness
- Absent bowel sounds suggest intra-abdominal catastrophe; tinkling/rushes suggest obstruction
- Dark urine (bilirubinuria) may precede jaundice in CBD obstruction
- Rigors (visible shaking chills) suggest bacteremia
- Pain out of proportion to exam is classic for acute mesenteric ischemia (absent in 20-25%)
- Orthostatic vital signs provide invaluable diagnostic and therapeutic information
- AAA rupture is misdiagnosed in 16% of cases; most common misdiagnosis is renal colic

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Unexplained hypotension with abdominal pain → AAA rupture, sepsis, ectopic pregnancy
- Peritoneal findings (rebound, rigidity, guarding) → perforation, AAA, ischemia
- Fever + jaundice + RUQ pain (Charcot triad) → ascending cholangitis (74% bacteremic)
- Pain out of proportion to exam → acute mesenteric ischemia
- Inability to pass stool or flatus → bowel obstruction; leukocytosis/acidosis = late sign of infarction
- Always check beta-HCG in premenopausal women with abdominal pain
