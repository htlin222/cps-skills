## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Three pivotal questions: (1) Monoarticular or polyarticular? (2) Articular or periarticular? (3) Inflammatory or noninflammatory? Further refine by acuity. All monoarticular arthritides can present polyarticularly and vice versa. Acute inflammatory monoarthritis is an absolute indication for arthrocentesis.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Gout | Most common inflammatory arthritis | First MTP, acute onset, hyperuricemia, male predominance | No |
| Septic arthritis (nongonococcal) | Must always consider | Subacute onset, fever (57%), S aureus most common | Yes |
| CPPD (pseudogout) | Common in elderly | Knee most common, chondrocalcinosis on XR, associated diseases | No |
| Osteoarthritis | Most common arthritis overall | Noninflammatory, weight-bearing joints, gradual onset | No |
| Rheumatoid arthritis | ~1% | Symmetric, small joints (MCPs, PIPs, wrists), morning stiffness >1h | No |
| Disseminated gonorrhea | Young, sexually active | Triad: tenosynovitis + dermatitis + arthralgia, or septic joint | Yes |
| Lyme disease | Endemic areas | Monoarticular knee, target rash, tick exposure | Yes |
| SLE | Uncommon | Polyarticular, systemic features, young women | No |
| Reactive arthritis | Uncommon | Post-enteric or urogenital infection, HLA-B27 | No |
| Psoriatic arthritis | ~0.1-0.25% | DIP involvement, dactylitis, nail changes, psoriasis | No |

## Gout Clinical Criteria (Rome/ARA)
Score 1 point each: (a) >1 attack of acute arthritis, (b) maximal inflammation <1 day, (c) monoarthritis, (d) joint erythema, (e) first MTP involvement, (f) unilateral MTP arthritis, (g) unilateral tarsal arthritis, (h) tophus, (i) asymmetric joint swelling, (j) hyperuricemia, (k) bone cysts without erosion on XR, (l) negative joint fluid culture

## Synovial Fluid Analysis Guide
| Parameter | Normal | Noninflammatory | Inflammatory | Septic |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|-------------|--------|
| WBC/mcL | <200 | 200-2,000 | 2,000-50,000 | >50,000 (often >100K) |
| PMN % | <25% | <25% | >50% | >75% |
| Clarity | Clear | Clear | Cloudy | Opaque |
| Diagnoses | - | OA, trauma | Crystal, RA, Lyme | Bacterial infection |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. **Monoarticular vs polyarticular**: Monoarticular = gout, pseudogout, septic, OA, trauma
2. **Articular vs periarticular**: ROM limited in all directions = articular; limited in one plane = periarticular
3. **Inflammatory vs noninflammatory**: Warmth, erythema, effusion, markedly limited ROM = inflammatory
4. **Acute inflammatory monoarthritis**: ALWAYS aspirate the joint
5. **Joint fluid**: Cell count + differential, Gram stain, culture, crystal analysis
6. **If septic suspected**: Blood cultures, start empiric antibiotics covering S aureus, urgent drainage
7. **Polyarticular inflammatory**: RF, anti-CCP, ANA, ESR/CRP; consider Lyme, viral, reactive

## Lyme Disease Stages
- **Early localized** (days-weeks): Erythema migrans (80% have rash; 60% homogeneous, 30% target, 10% multiple); myalgias (59%), fever (31%)
- **Early disseminated** (weeks-months): Multiple EM, cardiac (AV block), neurologic (Bell palsy, meningitis)
- **Late** (months-years): Monoarticular knee arthritis most common, chronic neurologic

## Clinical Pearls
- Every acute inflammatory joint effusion should be aspirated -- no exceptions
- Gout and septic arthritis can coexist -- positive crystals do NOT exclude infection
- Fever present in only 57% of septic arthritis; 43% are afebrile
- 44% of gout patients have fever; 10% have fever >39C
- Gout attacks follow abrupt uric acid changes (meals, alcohol, diuretics, urate-lowering therapy initiation)
- Only 30% of Lyme have classic target rash; 60% have homogeneous erythema
- Lyme transmission requires 36-48h tick attachment; no EM if removed <72h
- CPPD more commonly presents as chronic degenerative arthritis than acute pseudogout
- CPPD associated with: hyperparathyroidism, hemochromatosis, hypomagnesemia, hypophosphatasia
- Disseminated gonorrhea: women 3x more likely; often no history of recent STI
- Negative cultures do not exclude disseminated gonorrhea -- treat empirically if high suspicion
- Treatment within 5 days of symptom onset gives best prognosis for septic arthritis

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Acute hot monoarticular joint: assume septic until proven otherwise
- Septic arthritis delays >5 days dramatically worsen joint prognosis
- Prosthetic joint + fever/pain: septic arthritis until proven otherwise
- Polyarthritis + fever + new murmur: bacterial endocarditis
- Monoarticular knee in endemic area: Lyme disease
- Lower WBC cutoffs in synovial fluid are nondiagnostic -- only >100K has high LR+ for septic
