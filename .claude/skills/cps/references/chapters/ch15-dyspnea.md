# Ch15: Dyspnea

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Anatomic approach following inspired air through the cardiopulmonary system. Always consider the four most common serious causes first: HF, pneumonia, COPD/asthma, PE. Then use ACT ASAP mnemonic for life-threatening causes.

ACT ASAP: Anaphylaxis, Cardiac tamponade, Tension pneumothorax, Aortic dissection (acute AR), Status asthmaticus, Acute MI, Pulmonary embolism

Multiple causes often coexist (e.g., COPD + pneumonia, HF + atrial fibrillation).

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Heart failure (HFrEF/HFpEF) | Very common | Orthopnea, PND, JVD, S3, edema; prior MI or HTN | Mortality 20-50%/yr if symptomatic |
| Pneumonia | Common | Cough, fever, crackles, infiltrate on CXR | Sepsis, respiratory failure |
| COPD/Asthma | Common | Wheezing, prolonged expiration, tobacco history | Acute exacerbation |
| Pulmonary embolism | Must not miss | Risk factors (immobilization, surgery, cancer, estrogen); pleuritic pain | Can be fatal if missed |
| Atrial fibrillation | Common | Irregularly irregular pulse, palpitations | Stroke risk; rate/rhythm control |
| Mitral regurgitation | Common | Holosystolic murmur at apex radiating to axilla | Progressive LV dysfunction |
| Aortic regurgitation | Less common | Diastolic murmur at LSB; wide pulse pressure | Irreversible LV dysfunction if delayed |
| Aortic stenosis | Less common | Systolic murmur radiating to carotids; syncope | Sudden death |
| Pneumothorax | Less common | Sudden pleuritic pain; absent breath sounds | Tension pneumothorax |
| Anemia | Variable | Fatigue, pallor, tachycardia | Acute hemorrhage |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Assess severity: vital signs, oxygen saturation, work of breathing
2. History: risk factors for HF (prior MI, HTN, alcohol), COPD (tobacco), PE (immobilization, cancer)
3. Key pivotal question: is there chest pain? (pleuritic vs nonpleuritic, acute vs chronic)
   - Pleuritic + acute: PE, pneumonia, pneumothorax
   - Nonpleuritic + acute: MI, dissection, arrhythmia
4. Physical exam: JVD, S3, egophony, crackles, murmurs, edema
5. Basic workup for all: CXR, ECG, CBC
6. Based on clinical picture:
   - Suspected HF: BNP, echocardiogram
   - Suspected PE: D-dimer or CTA based on pretest probability
   - Suspected COPD/asthma: PFTs
7. POCUS increasingly useful as adjunct for HF, pneumothorax, effusion
8. Echocardiography recommended for all patients with known or suspected HF

## Clinical Pearls
- Signs of HF are commonly ABSENT even in advanced HF (42% of severe HF lack crackles, JVD, or edema)
- S3 in older non-pregnant patient is virtually pathognomonic of volume overload/HF
- S4 is NOT specific for HF; heard in many patients with LV hypertrophy
- BNP is also elevated in PE (avg 702 ng/L; 1876 in central PE)
- Unsuspected HF found in ~21% of COPD patients; low threshold for echo in COPD + dyspnea
- Normal CXR does NOT rule out HF, PE, COPD, or anemia
- Diastolic murmur is always abnormal and warrants echocardiography
- Systolic murmurs may be the ONLY murmur in aortic regurgitation (51% of mild-mod AR)
- Aortic regurgitation sensitivity varies dramatically by examiner (4-14% for non-cardiologists vs 80-95% for cardiologists)
- Multiple causes of dyspnea often coexist; do not stop at the first diagnosis
- HFrEF and HFpEF have similar mortality; treatment differs significantly

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- JVD + S3 + crackles + edema: decompensated HF; urgent diuresis needed
- New-onset atrial fibrillation: rule out MI (2-5% of new AF is acute MI), PE, thyrotoxicosis
- Pleuritic chest pain + dyspnea + risk factors: PE until proven otherwise
- Absent breath sounds + sudden onset: tension pneumothorax; needle decompression
- Diastolic murmur: always abnormal; do not delay echocardiography
- HF patients: high sodium diet associated with NNH ~3 for acute decompensation
- Symptomatic severe HF: up to 50% annual mortality; 50% die suddenly (VT/asystole)
- Atrial fibrillation: stroke risk 4.1%/yr without anticoagulation; up to 13%/yr with prior TIA/stroke
