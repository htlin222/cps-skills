# Ch21: Hematuria

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
First determine if true hematuria (vs pigmenturia or hemoglobinuria/myoglobinuria). Then distinguish glomerular vs nonglomerular via pivotal findings: dysmorphic RBCs (acanthocytes), RBC casts, proteinuria, hypertension, elevated creatinine suggest glomerular. Visible blood clots are NEVER glomerular -- indicate lower urinary tract source. Gross hematuria is always pathologic. Microscopic hematuria defined as >3 RBCs/hpf on >=2 specimens.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Bladder cancer | 20-25% of gross hematuria | Painless gross hematuria, male, smoker, age >40, industrial exposures | Yes |
| Nephrolithiasis | 21% of hematuria clinic pts | Colicky flank pain, nausea, hematuria | No |
| BPH | 12-13% of hematuria | Nocturia, hesitancy, weak stream, older males | No |
| IgA nephropathy | Most common primary GN | Synpharyngitic gross hematuria (within 12-72h of URI), proteinuria | Yes |
| Thin basement membrane nephropathy | Most common persistent hematuria cause | Isolated hematuria, family history, benign course | No |
| Renal cell carcinoma | 0.6-2% of hematuria | Hematuria + flank pain + mass (classic triad, now usually incidental) | Yes |
| Prostate cancer | 1.3-10% of gross hematuria | Often asymptomatic, PSA elevation, abnormal DRE | Yes |
| Infection-related GN | Less common | Hematuria + proteinuria + edema + hypertension post-infection | Yes |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Confirm hematuria: dipstick positive + RBCs on microscopy (rule out pigmenturia, hemoglobinuria, myoglobinuria)
2. Send urine culture in ALL patients with hematuria
3. Evaluate for glomerular markers: dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, proteinuria, elevated Cr, hypertension
4. If glomerular markers present -> nephrology referral; consider renal biopsy if proteinuria >500-1000 mg/day, elevated Cr, or hypertension
5. If nonglomerular + age >=40 or risk factors -> cystoscopy + CT urography (or ultrasound if CKD)
6. If age <40 without risk factors and glomerular features -> IgA nephropathy vs TBMN most likely
7. Patients >=40 or with visible clots require cystoscopy even if bleeding appears glomerular

## Clinical Pearls
- Gross hematuria is always pathologic and warrants full evaluation
- Malignancy extremely rare in patients <40 with microscopic hematuria
- Bladder cancer: male sex (3-4x risk), smoking (60% of male cases), age >40, industrial exposures
- IgA nephropathy: hematuria within 12-72h of mucosal infection (synpharyngitic) vs post-infectious GN (1-2 weeks after pharyngitis, 2-4 weeks after skin infection)
- TBMN: family history of hematuria in 30-50%; generally benign prognosis
- Visible hematuria episodes: TBMN 12%, Alport 33%, IgA nephropathy 88%
- RCC: 25% have metastatic or locally advanced disease at presentation
- IgA nephropathy: ESRD in up to 50% within 25 years; proteinuria >500-1000 mg/day predicts progression

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Painless gross hematuria + age >40 + smoking -> bladder cancer until proven otherwise -> cystoscopy
- Hematuria + proteinuria + rising creatinine + hypertension -> rapidly progressive GN -> urgent nephrology
- Gross hematuria + flank mass -> renal cell carcinoma -> CT with contrast
- Hematuria + edema + hypertension + recent infection -> infection-related GN
- Any patient >=40 with unexplained hematuria requires cystoscopy regardless of other findings
