## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Primary task: distinguish **serious** from **nonspecific mechanical** back pain using pivotal points:
1. **Neurologic signs/symptoms** → herniated disk, spinal stenosis, cauda equina
2. **Systemic disease clues** (red flags) → cancer, infection, fracture
3. **Visceral disease** → AAA, renal, GI, pelvic causes

Framework categories:
- A. Musculoskeletal: nonspecific mechanical (85-95%) vs specific (radiculopathy, stenosis, cauda equina)
- B. Systemic: neoplasia, infection, compression fracture, inflammatory arthritis
- C. Visceral: retroperitoneal, pelvic, renal, GI

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Mechanical back pain | 85-95% of back pain | Nonradiating, improves supine, 84% lifetime prevalence | Absence of ALL red flags = 99% nonserious |
| Herniated disk (L4-5, L5-S1) | 95% of disk herniations | Sciatica, worse sitting/coughing; 70% recover by 6 wks | Cauda equina syndrome |
| Spinal stenosis | 12-21% of older adults | Neurogenic claudication, relieved by flexion | Progressive cervical/thoracic stenosis → myelopathy |
| Metastatic cancer | <1% of all back pain | Constant, worse at night, not relieved by rest | Cancer + back pain + neuro abnormality = emergency |
| Compression fracture | Common in elderly | Acute onset, worsened by slight movements, T4 or below | Fracture above T4 → think malignancy |
| Spinal infection | Rare | Fever, IVDU, recent procedure, immunosuppression | Epidural abscess → emergent decompression |
| Cauda equina syndrome | Rare | Urinary retention, saddle anesthesia, bilateral sciatica | Medical emergency: immediate imaging + decompression |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Screen for **red flags** in ALL patients
2. If NO red flags → mechanical back pain; no imaging needed
3. If neurologic symptoms → assess for radiculopathy pattern (L4, L5, S1)
   - Cauda equina (urinary retention + saddle anesthesia + bilateral sciatica) → emergent MRI
4. If systemic disease suspected:
   - Cancer: MRI is best test; plain films first if no neuro deficits
   - Infection: MRI + inflammatory markers
   - Compression fracture: plain films; MRI to distinguish benign vs malignant
5. Spinal stenosis: clinical diagnosis; imaging for surgical planning only

## Neurogenic vs Vascular Claudication
| Feature | Neurogenic (Stenosis) | Vascular (PAD) |
|---------|----------------------|----------------|
| Pain distribution | Bilateral buttocks/thighs | Calves |
| Relieved by | Sitting, bending forward | Standing still |
| Worsened by | Standing, extension | Walking |
| Pulses | Normal | Diminished |
| Skin changes | None | Atrophic, hair loss |

## Clinical Pearls
- Absence of all red flags is 99% predictive of nonserious etiology
- A specific pathoanatomic diagnosis cannot be made in 85% of isolated low back pain
- Imaging does NOT improve outcomes in acute/subacute pain without red flags
- 75-90% of mechanical back pain improves within 1 month
- Bed rest prolongs pain; activity as tolerated is recommended
- MRI is best for cancer, cord compression, and distinguishing malignant from benign fractures
- Surgery for herniated disk: faster recovery (4 vs 12 weeks) but same outcomes at 1 year
- NSAIDs and muscle relaxants are effective for acute pain; acetaminophen is NOT effective

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Cancer: age >50, history of cancer, unexplained weight loss, failure of conservative therapy, nocturnal pain
- Cauda equina: urinary retention (LR+ 18), saddle anesthesia, bilateral sciatica, decreased anal tone
- Infection: fever, IVDU, immunosuppression, recent spinal procedure
- Fracture: age >70, corticosteroid use, significant trauma, osteoporosis
- Progressive neurologic deficit → urgent imaging regardless of other findings
- Fracture above T4 → more likely malignancy than osteoporosis
