## Differential Diagnosis Framework
First recognize shock (hypoperfusion). Classify by mechanism: distributive (62%), hypovolemic (16%), or cardiogenic (16%). Pivotal step: distinguish high-output (distributive/septic -- wide pulse pressure, warm extremities) from low-output (cardiogenic/hypovolemic -- narrow pulse pressure, cold extremities). Overlapping etiologies are common.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Septic shock | 62% of shock | Fever, wide pulse pressure, warm extremities, infection source | Yes |
| Hypovolemic shock | 16% | Hemorrhage or dehydration, narrow pulse pressure, cold extremities | Yes |
| Cardiogenic shock | 16% | JVD, S3, narrow pulse pressure, cold extremities, cardiac history | Yes |
| Pericardial tamponade | Rare | JVD, muffled sounds, pulsus paradoxus, recent surgery | Yes |
| Pulmonary embolism | Uncommon | RV dysfunction, hypoxia, pleuritic pain | Yes |

| Anaphylaxis | Uncommon | Urticaria, angioedema, allergen exposure | Yes |

## Cardiogenic Shock Specifics
- Complicates 5-8% of STEMI, 2.5% of non-STEMI (40,000-50,000 cases/year US)
- Mean LVEF in cardiogenic shock: 30%
- Mechanical complications (septal rupture, free wall rupture, papillary muscle): 12% of cases
- Ventricular septal rupture mortality: 87%
- Myocarditis causes cardiogenic shock in 10-15%
- Takotsubo cardiomyopathy: 4.2% progress to cardiogenic shock

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. **Recognize shock**: SBP <90 (or markedly reduced from baseline), tachycardia, altered mental status, decreased UOP, elevated lactate
2. **Assess pulse pressure**: Wide = high output (septic); Narrow = low output (cardiogenic/hypovolemic)
3. **Assess extremities**: Warm + bounding = septic; Cold + poor cap refill = cardiogenic/hypovolemic
4. **Differentiate cardiogenic vs hypovolemic**: JVD/S3/crackles = cardiogenic; flat veins + bleeding/dehydration = hypovolemic
5. **If unclear**: Bedside echo (LV function, pericardial effusion, RV dilation, IVC)
6. **Evaluate for occult sources**: Chest film (pneumonia, ARDS), UA (UTI), abdominal CT (abscess), catheter tips
7. **Overlapping etiologies common** (septic + cardiogenic most frequent overlap)

## Clinical Pearls
- Patient can be in shock with normal BP -- compare to prior readings in previously hypertensive patients
- Pulse pressure: wide (septic, high output) vs narrow (cardiogenic, hypovolemic, low output)
- WBC >15,000 only 28% sensitive for bacteremia; normal WBC does NOT rule it out
- Erythema at central catheter site: only 3% of catheter-related BSI
- Consider catheter infection even without local signs
- Bacteremia only 2% if ALL absent: temp >38.3, rigors, IVDU, acute abdomen, major comorbidity
- Admission hematocrit correlates poorly with acute blood loss; may be normal despite massive hemorrhage
- Shock + lactic acidosis: 70% mortality vs 25-35% without
- Appropriate antibiotics reduce sepsis mortality by 50%
- Sepsis sources commonly overlooked: sinusitis (with NG tube), acalculous cholecystitis, C difficile
- Anion gap only 44-67% sensitive for lactic acidosis; check serum lactate directly
- SOFA score >=2 increase defines sepsis; septic shock = sepsis + lactate >2 + vasopressors needed for MAP >=65

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Fever + rash: meningococcemia, RMSF, toxic shock syndrome -- evaluate immediately
- Sepsis with lactate >=4: 15% mortality even if normotensive
- Post-cardiac surgery + JVD + hypotension: tamponade until proven otherwise
- Refractory hypotension despite fluids/vasopressors: consider adrenal insufficiency
- Hypothermia (<35.5C) or leukopenia (<4000) in sepsis: predictors of mortality
- Temperature <37.6C does not rule out bacteremia (5% are normothermic, 13% hypothermic)
- Afebrile bacteremia associated with increased mortality
