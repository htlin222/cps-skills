# Ch22: Hypercalcemia

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Pivotal branch point: Is PTH elevated? Does patient have known malignancy?
- Ambulatory patients: primary hyperparathyroidism >90%
- Hospitalized patients: malignancy most common

Three clinical settings: (1) asymptomatic on routine labs, (2) evaluation of symptoms (constipation, fatigue, nephrolithiasis), (3) severe hypercalcemia with altered mental status.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence/Setting | Key Features |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------|
| Primary hyperparathyroidism | >90% ambulatory; 85% solitary adenoma | Chronic mild hypercalcemia, elevated PTH |
| Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy | Most common inpatient | Known cancer, elevated PTHrP, low PTH; 50% 30-day mortality |
| Local osteolytic metastasis | Breast cancer, myeloma | Bone pain, lytic lesions, anemia |
| Milk-alkali syndrome | 3rd most common inpatient | CaCO3 ingestion + metabolic alkalosis + AKI |
| FHH | Autosomal dominant | Mild chronic hypercalcemia, normal/slight elevated PTH, low urine Ca |
| Thiazide-induced | Common | Mild; PTH normal or elevated in 98% |
| Granulomatous disease | Sarcoidosis, lymphoma | Calcitriol-mediated |
| Vitamin D toxicity | Excessive supplementation | Elevated 25-OH vitamin D, low PTH |

## Pivotal Findings & Likelihood Ratios
| Finding | Sens | Spec | LR+ | LR- | Target Dx |
|---------|------|------|-----|-----|-----------|
| Elevated PTH + hypercalcemia | ~90% | ~99% | ~90 | 0.10 | Primary hyperparathyroidism (10-20% have "normal" PTH) |
| Low PTH + elevated PTHrP | 80% | >99% | >80 | 0.20 | Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy |
| Fractional excretion Ca <0.01 | ~95% | ~90% | ~9.5 | 0.06 | FHH (urine Ca <100 mg/day) |
| Ca normalizes after stopping thiazide | 33% | ~99% | ~33 | 0.68 | Thiazide-induced (only 1/3 normalize) |
| Abnormal SPEP | 82% | -- | -- | -- | Plasma cell myeloma |
| UPEP (if SPEP normal) | 97% of SPEP-neg | -- | -- | -- | Light chain myeloma |
| Parathyroidectomy -> normocalcemia | 95-98% | -- | -- | -- | Confirms primary hyperparathyroidism |

PTHrP detectable in 80% of patients with hypercalcemia of malignancy. Most common tumors: squamous cell carcinomas and adenocarcinomas (lung, pancreas, kidney).

Diagnostic criteria: marrow plasmacytosis >10% + CRAB or biomarkers. MGUS: ~3% of patients >50; ~1%/year progression.

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Confirm hypercalcemia: corrected Ca = total Ca + 0.8 x (4 - albumin) or check ionized calcium
2. Check PTH -- KEY branch point
3. PTH elevated -> primary hyperparathyroidism (most likely); exclude FHH (FE Ca <0.01, family hx), lithium, thiazide, CKD
4. PTH low -> check PTHrP: if elevated -> HHM; if low -> osteolytic metastasis, vitamin D excess, granulomatous disease, milk-alkali
5. Thiazide use: stop and recheck in 1 week; only 1/3 normalize; 80% of persistent = primary hyperparathyroidism

## Surgery Indications for Primary Hyperparathyroidism
- Symptomatic hypercalcemia
- Serum Ca >1 mg/dL above normal
- CrCl <60 mL/min
- Nephrolithiasis, nephrocalcinosis, or hypercalciuria
- T-score <-2.5 or fragility fracture
- Age <50; patient preference
- 10-year data: 73% asymptomatic patients had no progression without surgery
- Parathyroidectomy: 95-98% normocalcemia, 100% bone density improvement, 82% symptom improvement

## Treatment of Severe Hypercalcemia
| Step | Agent | Onset | Mechanism |
|------|-------|-------|-----------|
| 1 | IV normal saline | Immediate | Treats hypovolemia, increases Ca excretion |
| 2 | Loop diuretic (after hydration) | Hours | Calciuresis once euvolemic |
| 3 | Calcitonin | Hours | Rapid but tachyphylaxis at ~48h |
| 4 | Bisphosphonate (zoledronic acid) | 48 hours | Inhibits osteoclasts; definitive |

## Clinical Pearls
- Primary hyperparathyroidism: 85% solitary adenoma; most now asymptomatic
- Hypercalcemia of malignancy: most have PREVIOUSLY DIAGNOSED cancer when Ca rises
- Milk-alkali triad: hypercalcemia + metabolic alkalosis + AKI; modern cause = calcium carbonate
- FHH: autosomal dominant; benign; FE Ca <0.01 distinguishes from hyperparathyroidism (LR+ ~9.5)
- Thiazide hypercalcemia discovered ~5 years after initiation on average
- Myeloma lytic lesions MISSED on bone scan; use plain XR or low-dose CT
- Light chain myeloma: normal SPEP and dipstick; need UPEP and free light chains

## Red Flags
- Severe hypercalcemia (Ca >14) + altered mental status -> emergency: IV saline + calcitonin + bisphosphonate
- Hypercalcemia + weight loss + hepatomegaly -> malignancy (PTHrP, PTH, imaging)
- Hypercalcemia + bone pain + anemia + renal insufficiency -> myeloma (SPEP/UPEP, bone survey, marrow bx)
- New hypercalcemia in known cancer -> HHM (50% 30-day mortality)
