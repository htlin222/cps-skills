# Ch10: Cough, Fever, and Respiratory Infections

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Two pivotal questions: (1) Does the patient warrant a chest radiograph for pneumonia? (2) If pneumonia is present, is it CAP vs other type (PJP, aspiration, TB)?

Framework: Common cold > Sinusitis > Bronchitis > Influenza > Pertussis > Pneumonia (CAP, hospital-acquired, aspiration, TB, PJP)

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Common cold | Very common | Rhinitis, sore throat, low-grade fever | -- |
| Acute bronchitis | Common | Cough 1-3 wk, low-grade fever; bacteria <10% | -- |
| Influenza | Seasonal | Abrupt onset, high fever peaking in 12-24h, myalgias, cough | Pneumonia complication |
| CAP | Common | Cough, fever, infiltrate on CXR | Sepsis, respiratory failure |
| Aspiration pneumonia | 5-15% of PNA | Risk: neurologic impairment, alcohol, sedation; putrid sputum | Abscess, empyema |
| TB | Uncommon in US | Chronic cough, weight loss, night sweats; upper lobe/cavitary | Public health emergency |
| PJP | Immunocompromised | Subacute dyspnea, dry cough; bilateral diffuse infiltrates | AIDS-defining; mortality 30-60% in non-HIV |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Cough + congestion: assess for pneumonia red flags (dyspnea, high fever, altered MS, hypoxia, abnormal lung exam)
2. Red flags present: CXR; if infiltrate, pneumonia confirmed
3. No red flags + normal vitals + normal lung exam + low risk: no CXR needed
4. If pneumonia: determine type via risk factors + CXR pattern
   - Lower/middle lobe: CAP (most common; 94% of CAP infiltrates)
   - Upper lobe/cavitary: TB or aspiration
   - Bilateral diffuse: PJP, Mycoplasma, influenza
5. Influenza season + fever + cough: consider influenza testing/empiric therapy
6. CURB-65 score guides disposition (Confusion, Uremia, RR>=30, BP<90, age>=65)

## Clinical Pearls
- Normal lung exam does NOT rule out pneumonia (LR- only 0.6)
- Elderly with PNA often lack fever (only 53% febrile)
- Purulent sputum is NOT an indication for antibiotics in acute bronchitis
- PA + lateral CXR superior to single AP view (90% vs 59% sensitivity)
- CAP rarely affects upper lobes; consider TB or aspiration if upper lobe
- Positive viral PCR does NOT exclude bacterial CAP (coinfection 19-39%)
- 3.4% of pneumonias have underlying malignancy; follow-up CXR needed
- High fever (>38C) increasing over days suggests bacterial PNA, not influenza
- DLCO >75% predicted makes PJP likelihood <2%
- PJP can present without known HIV; often the presenting illness of AIDS
- Clinical diagnosis of influenza: sens 36%, spec 78% -- poor sensitivity

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Dyspnea, high fever, hypoxia, altered mental status: get CXR urgently
- Bilateral diffuse infiltrates + subacute course: consider PJP even without known HIV
- Cavitary disease, upper lobe, chronic symptoms: TB until proven otherwise; isolate patient
- Influenza + pneumonia: suspect/treat bacterial superinfection (S. pneumoniae, MRSA)
- PaO2 <70 in PJP: corticosteroids are lifesaving
- CURB-65 >=1: consider hospitalization for CAP
