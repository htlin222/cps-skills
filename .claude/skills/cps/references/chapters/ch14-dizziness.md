# Ch14: Dizziness

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
First pivotal step: classify the type of dizziness by asking "What does it feel like?"
- Vertigo (spinning/motion) -- most common
- Near syncope (feeling faint)
- Dysequilibrium (imbalance/falling sensation)
- Ill-defined light-headedness (cannot classify)

For vertigo, second pivotal step: central vs peripheral, then duration/trigger pattern.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| BPPV | 17-42% of vertigo | Brief (<1 min), triggered by head position, clusters over weeks | -- |
| Vestibular neuritis | Common | Continuous vertigo for days, spontaneous, nausea; hearing preserved | Must distinguish from stroke |
| Meniere disease | Less common | Recurrent episodes (min-hours), tinnitus, hearing loss, aural fullness | -- |
| Vestibular migraine | Common | Vertigo sec-hours; history of migraines; photophobia/phonophobia | New headache + vertigo = red flag |
| Cerebellar/brainstem stroke | Must not miss | Acute vestibular syndrome with CNS signs; risk factors for CVD | 34% misdiagnosed; life-threatening |
| Cerebellar hemorrhage | Emergency | Abrupt headache + vomiting + ataxia + vertigo; rapid deterioration | Herniation and death (42%) |
| Vertebral artery dissection | Must not miss | Young patient; headache + neck pain + vertigo; trauma/manipulation | Leading cause posterior stroke in young |
| Multiple sclerosis | 5% presenting Sx | Young adults; vertigo + other CNS symptoms disseminated in time/space | -- |
| Nonspecific/psychiatric dizziness | 20-38% | Continuous, poorly defined; depression, anxiety, panic disorder | Must exclude organic causes first |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Classify dizziness type: vertigo vs near syncope vs dysequilibrium vs ill-defined
2. If vertigo, step 1: CNS signs/symptoms? (headache, diplopia, dysarthria, ataxia, cranial nerve abnormalities)
   - If yes: MRI urgently
3. If vertigo without obvious CNS signs, step 2: duration + trigger
   - Brief (<1 min) + triggered by head position = BPPV; confirm with Dix Hallpike
   - Minutes to hours + spontaneous = TIA, vestibular migraine, or Meniere
   - Continuous for days + spontaneous = acute vestibular syndrome: do HINTS exam
4. Acute vestibular syndrome: HINTS exam is critical
   - All peripheral findings (abnormal head impulse, unidirectional nystagmus, no skew) = vestibular neuritis
   - Any central finding (normal head impulse, bidirectional nystagmus, or skew) = stroke/CNS; MRI urgently
5. Negative MRI does NOT rule out stroke if HINTS is positive; repeat MRI
6. Vertigo + headache/neck pain: consider cerebellar hemorrhage, VAD; emergent imaging

## Clinical Pearls
- HINTS exam is more sensitive than initial DW-MRI for brainstem stroke
- 25% of acute vestibular syndrome patients have stroke, many without obvious CNS signs
- 34% of cerebellar strokes are misdiagnosed; most commonly as vestibular neuritis
- Vertigo exacerbated by motion is NOT pathognomonic of BPPV -- almost all causes worsen with motion
- Duration of each individual episode (not total illness duration) narrows the DDx dramatically
- VAD: median age 43; accounts for 42% of posterior strokes in patients <50
- Chiropractic manipulation: symptoms develop within 1 hour in 83% of VAD cases
- In VBI, 90-day recurrent stroke rate is 13.9% with stenosis vs 2.8% without
- Depressed patients may have organic causes of dizziness; always examine carefully
- Epley maneuver is 56% effective for BPPV

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Vertigo + new severe headache or neck pain: cerebellar hemorrhage, SAH, or VAD
- Normal head impulse test in acute vestibular syndrome: suggests stroke (LR+ 17)
- Bidirectional nystagmus or vertical skew deviation: central lesion
- Inability to ambulate or sit unaided: suggests cerebellar stroke
- Vertigo + any focal neurologic finding: MRI urgently
- Young patient + vertigo + neck pain + recent trauma/manipulation: VAD
- Cerebellar hemorrhage: rapid progression; 28-38% show hematoma expansion within 3 hours
