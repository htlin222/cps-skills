## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Systematic 7-step approach:
1. **pH** -> acidosis (<7.4) vs alkalosis (>7.4)
2. **HCO3- and PaCO2** -> metabolic vs respiratory
3. **Anion gap** (for metabolic acidosis) -> gap vs non-gap
4. **Explore DDx** of the primary disorder
5. **Diagnose** primary disorder
6. **Check for additional disorders**: (A) Always calculate anion gap even without acidosis; (B) Check compensation
7. **Final diagnosis** integrating all disorders

Anion gap = Na+ - (HCO3- + Cl-); normal 12 +/- 4 (institution-dependent; some 7-9)
Albumin correction: reduce normal AG by 2.5 mEq/L per 1 g/dL drop in albumin below 4.4
Osmolar gap = measured osmolality - calculated osmolality; normal <10 mOsm/kg
Calculated osmolality = (2 x Na) + Glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 + ETOH/3.7

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence/Setting | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------|---------------|
| DKA | 4.6-8.0/1000 person-yr in DM | Glucose >250, pH <=7.3, HCO3 <=18, ketones+, AG >10 | Mortality 5-15%; precipitant is #1 cause of death |
| Lactic acidosis | Most common metabolic acidosis in hospitalized | Hypoperfusion, sepsis, shock; trace O2 pathway | Mortality 70% with shock + lactic acidosis vs 25-35% without |
| Alcoholic ketoacidosis | Advanced alcoholism | Normal-low glucose, NADH elevation; mild-profound | Concomitant lactic acidosis common; check osmolar gap for toxic ingestion |
| Uremic acidosis | CKD with Cr >4-5 mg/dL | HCO3 stabilizes 12-20; early=non-gap, late=gap | Sulfates, phosphates, urate accumulate |
| Starvation ketosis | Diminished carbohydrate intake | Mild ketosis (HCO3 >=14), normal glucose, normal pH | Rarely clinically significant alone |
| Respiratory acidosis | Severe lung/neuromuscular disease | PaCO2 >45; acute vs chronic key distinction | Normal PaCO2 in asthma = impending respiratory failure |
| Metabolic alkalosis | Vomiting, diuretics, contraction | Elevated HCO3, low Cl; saline-responsive vs resistant | Requires both generation AND maintenance mechanism |
| Toxic ingestions | Methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates | Elevated osmolar gap (methanol, EG); AG acidosis | Check osmolar gap in all unexplained AG acidosis |

## Compensation Formulas
| Primary Disorder | Expected Compensation |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Metabolic acidosis | PaCO2 drops 1.2 mmHg per 1 mEq/L fall in HCO3 |
| Metabolic alkalosis | PaCO2 rises 0.7 mmHg per 1 mEq/L rise in HCO3 |
| Acute respiratory acidosis | HCO3 rises 1 mEq/L per 10 mmHg rise in PaCO2 |
| Chronic respiratory acidosis | HCO3 rises 4 mEq/L per 10 mmHg rise in PaCO2 |
| Acute respiratory alkalosis | HCO3 falls 2 mEq/L per 10 mmHg fall in PaCO2 |
| Chronic respiratory alkalosis | HCO3 falls 5 mEq/L per 10 mmHg fall in PaCO2 |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Check pH -> if <7.4: acidosis; if >7.4: alkalosis
2. Identify primary: low HCO3 = metabolic acidosis; high PaCO2 = respiratory acidosis; high HCO3 = metabolic alkalosis; low PaCO2 = respiratory alkalosis
3. If metabolic acidosis -> calculate anion gap (correct for albumin)
   - Elevated AG: DKA, lactic acidosis, uremia, toxins (methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates)
   - Normal AG: diarrhea, RTA (types I, II, IV), early CKD
4. **Always check AG** even without acidosis -- hidden AG acidosis masked by concurrent metabolic alkalosis
5. Check compensation against formulas -> deviation = additional disorder present
6. Delta-delta ratio: compare rise in AG to fall in HCO3
   - Ratio ~1.6:1 in pure lactic acidosis
   - Ratio ~1:1 in pure ketoacidosis
   - Ratio >2: concurrent metabolic alkalosis
   - Ratio <1: concurrent non-gap metabolic acidosis
7. If elevated osmolar gap -> consider methanol, ethylene glycol, isopropanol ingestion

## DKA Diagnostic Criteria (ADA)
- Glucose >250 mg/dL (euglycemic DKA: SGLT2 inhibitors [mean glucose 265], pregnancy, poor oral intake)
- pH <=7.3
- HCO3 <=18 mEq/L
- Positive serum ketones (beta-hydroxybutyrate preferred)
- Anion gap >10 mEq/L

## DKA Key Treatment Principles
- IV normal saline 1-2 L bolus -> then 500 mL/h x 4h -> 250 mL/h x 4h
- Insulin: IV bolus 0.1 units/kg then 0.1 units/kg/h (or skip bolus, start 0.14 units/kg/h)
- **Do NOT start insulin if K <3.3 mEq/L** -- replace potassium first
- Continue IV insulin until: glucose <200 AND 2 of: AG <=12, HCO3 >=15, venous pH >7.3
- Add D5W/D10W if glucose <200 but AG not yet normalized -- do NOT stop insulin
- Give SQ insulin 1-2 hours before stopping IV insulin to prevent rebound ketoacidosis
- HCO3 therapy: only if pH <6.9 (not shown to help if pH >6.9)
- Monitor K hourly; replace when <5.0-5.2 and urine output present

## Clinical Pearls
- Always check the anion gap, even when HCO3 is normal or high -> hidden AG acidosis
- Normal PaCO2 during asthma attack = respiratory fatigue = impending respiratory failure
- PaCO2 >=40 mmHg during metabolic acidosis = inappropriate compensation = respiratory failure
- 12-47% of DKA cases occur in type 2 DM; more common in Black and Hispanic patients
- SGLT2 inhibitors cause euglycemic DKA; maintain high suspicion especially perioperatively
- Serum creatinine may be falsely elevated in DKA due to ketone interference with assay
- Effective osmolality = (2 x Na) + Glucose/18; altered mental status expected if >320 mOsm/L
- Mental status change with osmolality <320 -> search for neurologic insult (CVA, drug intoxication)
- A normal AG does NOT exclude lactic acidosis; always measure lactate in critically ill patients
- Metformin-associated lactic acidosis: hold if GFR <30; reduce dose if GFR 30-45; hold 48h after IV contrast
- Alcoholic ketoacidosis treatment: give IV thiamine BEFORE glucose to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy
- Sodium correction in DKA: Na rises 2.4 mEq/L per 100 mg/dL fall in glucose with treatment
- Despite hyperkalemia at presentation, total body K deficit in DKA is 3-5 mEq/kg

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Lactic acidosis in setting of infection -> sepsis with tissue hypoperfusion (mortality 70% with shock)
- pH <6.9 in DKA -> consider HCO3 therapy (only indication)
- Hypokalemia developing during DKA treatment -> life-threatening; delay insulin if K <3.3
- Normal or low K on DKA presentation -> higher risk of life-threatening hypokalemia during treatment; cardiac monitoring mandatory
- PaCO2 >=40 in metabolic acidosis -> respiratory failure, not compensation
- Elevated osmolar gap + AG acidosis -> toxic ingestion (methanol, ethylene glycol) until proven otherwise
- MI as precipitant of DKA -> always check ECG and troponin; precipitant is #1 cause of DKA mortality
