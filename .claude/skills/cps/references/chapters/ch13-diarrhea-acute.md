# Ch13: Diarrhea, Acute

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Categorize by duration: acute (<14 days), persistent (14-29 days), chronic (>=30 days). Then subcategorize acute diarrhea into infectious vs noninfectious. Infectious further divided by clinical pattern: gastroenteritis (large volume, watery) vs colitis (small volume, bloody, tenesmus).

Key factors: (1) associated symptoms, (2) duration, (3) exposure history, (4) immunosuppression, (5) pathogen epidemiology

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Norovirus | Most common viral | Acute vomiting then diarrhea; 1-3 days; winter peak; highly contagious | Outbreaks in institutions |
| Toxin-mediated (S. aureus, C. perfringens, B. cereus) | ~1% foodborne illness | 1-16h after ingestion; vomiting predominant; rapid recovery 12-48h | -- |
| Salmonella | 1.23M cases/yr US | Eggs/poultry; fever; watery or bloody; bacteremia in 5% | Typhoid fever in travelers |
| Shigella | Most common cause bloody diarrhea US | Dysentery (bloody + tenesmus); sexual transmission (MSM outbreaks) | Antibiotic resistance increasing |
| Campylobacter | 2nd most common bloody diarrhea | Fever precedes GI Sx; abdominal pain; self-limited 4-6 days | Guillain-Barre (rare) |
| STEC (E. coli O157:H7) | Less common | Bloody diarrhea + abdominal pain WITHOUT fever | HUS (hemolytic uremic syndrome) |
| C. difficile | 10-20% of antibiotic-associated | Antibiotic exposure; healthcare; watery to bloody; leukocytosis | Toxic megacolon, perforation |
| Giardia | Most common parasite US | Bloating, flatulence, greasy stools; water exposure; fever uncommon | Immunodeficiency if recurrent |
| Lactose intolerance (post-infectious) | 50-70% post-gastroenteritis | Bloating, flatulence, diarrhea with dairy; after GI infection | -- |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Confirm diarrhea (>=3 loose stools/day) and duration (<14 days = acute)
2. Assess for noninfectious causes: medications, supplements, dietary (sorbitol, lactose)
3. Characterize pattern: gastroenteritis (watery, vomiting) vs colitis (bloody, tenesmus)
4. Most acute diarrhea is self-limited; testing NOT needed in most immunocompetent patients
5. Test if: bloody diarrhea, severe illness (dehydration, high fever), immunosuppressed, healthcare/food worker, outbreak, prolonged (>7 days), recent antibiotics
6. Testing: multipathogen molecular panel preferred; reflex culture for susceptibility when needed
7. Do NOT use antidiarrheals (loperamide) in dysentery or suspected C. difficile

## Clinical Pearls
- Only 2-15% of bacterial stool tests are positive in resource-rich settings
- Organisms causing dysentery (Shigella, Campylobacter, STEC) are at least as likely to cause non-bloody diarrhea
- Diarrhea as antibiotic side effect complicates up to 25% of antibiotic courses
- Post-gastroenteritis lactose intolerance: 50-70% incidence; advise dairy avoidance for 2 weeks
- Antibiotics do NOT shorten Salmonella carriage duration; may paradoxically prolong it
- STEC: antibiotics controversial and may increase HUS risk
- Community-associated C. difficile rising (up to 41% of cases in some areas)
- Typhoid fever: suspect in returning traveler with fever; diarrhea not always present
- Incubation period helps identify pathogen: hours = toxin, 1-2 days = viral, >=24h = bacterial

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Bloody diarrhea + no fever: think STEC; avoid antibiotics (HUS risk)
- Bloody diarrhea + high fever + tenesmus: Shigella most likely; treat with antibiotics
- Recent antibiotics + diarrhea + leukocytosis: C. difficile; test with PCR
- Orthostatic hypotension: significant dehydration; may need IV fluids
- Returning traveler with fever: consider typhoid (Salmonella typhi/paratyphi)
- Immunosuppressed patient: consider opportunistic pathogens (Cryptosporidium, CMV, MAC)
- Never use antidiarrheals in dysentery or C. difficile: risk of toxic megacolon
