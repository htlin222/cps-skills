# Ch1: Diagnostic Process

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Clinical Reasoning Framework (9-Step Model)
1. **Identify the problem**: Clarify chief complaint; construct complete problem list; group related problems
2. **Frame the differential**: Use problem-specific frameworks (anatomic, organ/system, physiologic, pivotal points)
3. **Organize the differential**: Restructure using pivotal points (opposing descriptors: old vs new, unilateral vs bilateral)
4. **Limit the differential**: Extract patient-specific pivotal points from H&P to narrow the list
5. **Explore possible diagnoses**: Look for clinical clues, risk factors, "fingerprint" findings
6. **Rank the differential**: Use probabilistic + prognostic + pragmatic approaches simultaneously
7. **Test your hypotheses**: Understand how tests change probability via LR
8. **Re-rank based on new data**: Update differential after test results
9. **Determine final diagnosis**: Reach sufficient certainty or identify next steps

## Dual Reasoning Systems
- **System 1**: Rapid, intuitive pattern recognition (illness scripts). Experienced clinicians, straightforward cases
- **System 2**: Slow, analytical, explicit reasoning. Complex cases or less experienced clinicians
- Most reasoning = unconscious blending of both. Watch for cognitive biases

## Common Biases (Table 1-1)
Anchoring, availability, premature closure, confirmation bias, base rate neglect

## Value of Physical Exam Findings
| Metric | % | Implication |
|--------|---|-------------|
| Positive findings with specificity >80% | 65% | Most positives are useful |
| Positive findings with specificity >90% | 43% | Nearly half highly specific |
| Positive findings with LR+ >5 | 33% | One-third moderately diagnostic |
| Positive findings with LR+ >10 | 16% | Strong diagnostic power |
| Negative findings with sensitivity >80% | 21% | Most negatives NOT useful for ruling out |
| Negative findings with LR- <0.1 | 7% | Very few negatives strongly rule out |

**Key lesson**: Focus on the positive. Do NOT be fooled by the negative — classic findings are often absent.

## Likelihood Ratio Interpretation
| LR+ | Shift | Clinical Utility |
|-----|-------|-----------------|
| >10 | Large increase in probability | Very useful for ruling IN |
| 5-10 | Moderate increase | Somewhat useful |
| 2-5 | Small increase | Marginally useful |
| 1 | No change | Useless test |

| LR- | Shift | Clinical Utility |
|-----|-------|-----------------|
| <0.1 | Large decrease in probability | Very useful for ruling OUT |
| 0.1-0.2 | Moderate decrease | Somewhat useful |
| 0.2-0.5 | Small decrease | Marginally useful |

## Pretest Probability Rules (Table 1-5)
- LR- of 0.1 or less → rules out disease of low or moderate pretest probability
- LR+ of 10 or greater → rules in disease of moderate or high probability
- **High pretest probability + negative test → rarely rules out** (no matter what LR-)
- **Low pretest probability + positive test → rarely rules in** (no matter what LR+)

## Ranking the Differential (Table 1-2)
| Approach | Method | Limitation |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Possibilistic | All equally likely | NOT useful |
| Probabilistic | Highest pretest probability first | May miss rare dangerous diagnoses |
| Prognostic | Most serious first | May over-test low-probability conditions |
| Pragmatic | Most treatable first | May delay diagnosis of untreatable conditions |

**Best practice**: Simultaneously integrate all three. Identify:
1. **Leading hypothesis** (most likely)
2. **Must-not-miss hypotheses** (dangerous, regardless of probability)
3. **Other active alternatives** (plausible diagnoses to keep on differential)

## "Fingerprint" Findings (FP)
Findings so specific they are rarely seen without the disease. Marked with FP symbol throughout textbook. These have very high LR+ values and often clinch the diagnosis.
