# Ch33: Wheezing and Stridor

> Clinical data compiled from primary literature. See references.bib for sources.

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Essential first distinction: wheezing (expiratory, distal airways) vs stridor (inspiratory, large airways -- emergency). Use anatomic approach from upper airway to distal airways. Patients often describe both as wheezing; physical exam is essential to differentiate. Stridor = impending airway obstruction = emergency.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Asthma | Most common cause of wheezing | Intermittent, reversible, triggers, nocturnal worsening | No |
| COPD | Common | Smoking history, persistent, progressive, older age | No |
| Pulmonary edema/HF | Common | Dyspnea, orthopnea, JVD, edema, bilateral crackles | Yes |
| Anaphylaxis/laryngeal edema | Uncommon | Acute stridor, urticaria, allergen exposure, hypotension | Yes |
| Epiglottitis | Rare (adults) | Stridor, drooling, fever, dysphagia, muffled voice | Yes |
| Foreign body aspiration | Uncommon | Acute onset, unilateral wheeze, choking history | Yes |
| PVCM (vocal cord dysfunction) | Underrecognized | Inspiratory stridor mimicking asthma, normal PFTs between episodes | No |
| Pulmonary embolism | Uncommon | Acute dyspnea, pleuritic pain, wheezing possible | Yes |
| Bronchitis | Common | Cough with sputum, self-limited, no chronic airflow obstruction | No |
| Tracheal stenosis | Rare | Prior intubation, progressive stridor, fixed obstruction | No |

## Stridor Differential by Anatomy
| Level | Causes |
|-------|--------|
| Nasopharynx/oropharynx | Tonsillar hypertrophy, peritonsillar abscess, retropharyngeal abscess |
| Larynx | Epiglottitis, PVCM, anaphylaxis, tumors, vocal cord paralysis |
| Trachea | Tracheal stenosis, tracheomalacia, goiter |
| Proximal airways | Foreign body, caustic ingestion/inhalation, burns |

## Pivotal Findings & Likelihood Ratios
| Finding | Value | Target Diagnosis | Notes |
|---------|-------|-----------------|-------|
| Wheezing episodes (history) | LR+ 2.6 | Asthma | Sens 36%, Spec 86% |
| Nighttime cough | LR+ 2.5 | Asthma | Sens 40%, Spec 84% |
| Nighttime chest tightness | LR+ 2.3 | Asthma | Sens 46%, Spec 80% |
| Cough during/after exercise | 90% specificity | Asthma | Methacholine challenge study |
| PEF variability >20% | Diagnostic | Asthma | Diurnal variation (lowest AM, highest midday) |
| FEV1 reversibility (>200 mL and >12%) | Diagnostic | Asthma | Post-bronchodilator |
| Methacholine challenge (<20% FEV1 drop) | 95% NPV | Excludes asthma | When suspicious history + normal PFTs |
| Stridor (inspiratory) | Emergency | Large airway obstruction | Evaluate immediately |

## Asthma Classification
| Severity | Symptoms | Nighttime | SABA Use | FEV1 |
|----------|----------|-----------|----------|------|
| Intermittent | <=2 days/week | <=2x/month | <=2 days/week | >80% |
| Mild persistent | >2 days/week | 3-4x/month | >2 days/week | >80% |
| Moderate persistent | Daily | >1x/week | Daily | 60-80% |
| Severe persistent | Throughout day | Often nightly | Several x/day | <60% |

## Stepwise Asthma Treatment
1. **Step 1** (intermittent): SABA PRN
2. **Step 2**: Low-dose ICS
3. **Step 3**: Low-dose ICS + LABA (or medium-dose ICS)
4. **Step 4**: Medium-dose ICS + LABA
5. **Step 5**: High-dose ICS + LABA; consider omalizumab (allergic)
6. **Step 6**: High-dose ICS + LABA + oral corticosteroids
- SABA use >2x/week: consider stepping up
- Always try to step down when control achieved

## Exacerbation Severity
| Severity | Symptoms | FEV1/PEF |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Mild | With activity only | >=70% predicted |
| Moderate | With usual activities | 40-69% predicted |
| Severe | At rest, interferes with speech | <40% predicted |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. **Wheezing vs stridor?** Physical exam essential
2. **Stridor**: Emergency -- epiglottitis, anaphylaxis, foreign body, tracheal stenosis, PVCM
3. **Wheezing**: Asthma most likely; consider COPD, HF, PE, bronchitis
4. **Asthma diagnosis**: Clinical (episodic symptoms + reversible airflow obstruction + exclusion of alternatives)
   - Spirometry: decreased FEV1, decreased FEV1/FVC, reversibility
   - Normal PFTs + suspicious history: methacholine challenge (95% NPV)
5. **Refractory asthma**: Check adherence, inhaler technique, untreated GERD/sinusitis/allergies, incorrect diagnosis (PVCM, COPD, sarcoid, eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis, ABPA)

## Clinical Pearls
- Absence of wheezing does NOT exclude asthma -- cough-variant asthma is common
- Asthma worse at night (decreased mucociliary clearance, low catecholamines) and with exercise (airway cooling/drying)
- Decreased wheezing + worsening distress = DANGER (insufficient airflow to generate wheeze)
- Decreased wheezing + lessened distress = improvement
- PVCM frequently confused with asthma; diagnose with laryngoscopy during symptoms
- Asthma patients describe dyspnea as chest tightness more than other cardiorespiratory diseases
- Viral infections are most common trigger for asthma exacerbations
- Asthma can present for the first time in adulthood
- COPD: fixed obstruction (no reversibility); asthma: variable obstruction
- Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction may be only manifestation of asthma
- Occupational asthma: consider in new-onset adult asthma (chemical/dust exposure)

## Risk Factors for Asthma-Related Death
- Previous severe exacerbation or intubation
- Multiple recent ED visits or hospitalizations
- >2 SABA canisters per month
- Current or recent systemic corticosteroids
- Difficulty perceiving airflow obstruction
- Low socioeconomic status, inner-city residence
- Illicit drug use
- Comorbid medical or psychiatric disease

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- Stridor: impending airway obstruction -- treat as emergency
- Acute wheezing + hypotension + urticaria: anaphylaxis
- New-onset wheezing in adult without history: HF, PE, foreign body, endobronchial lesion
- Worsening distress + silent chest: critical airflow obstruction -- impending respiratory arrest
- FEV1 or PEF <40% during exacerbation: severe -- consider ICU admission
- Any patient with risk factors for asthma death + exacerbation: seriously consider hospitalization
