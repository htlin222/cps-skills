# Ch17: Edema

## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Distribution is the pivotal point: bilateral leg edema -> systemic causes first (cardiac, hepatic, renal); unilateral -> venous/lymphatic causes. Pathophysiology: increased capillary hydrostatic pressure, increased capillary permeability, decreased plasma oncotic pressure, increased interstitial oncotic pressure, lymphatic obstruction. Edema not clinically apparent until interstitial volume increases by 2.5-3 L.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Heart failure | Very common | Dyspnea, JVD, S3, pulmonary crackles, bilateral edema | Yes |
| Cirrhosis/portal hypertension | Common | Ascites, splenomegaly, spider angiomata, thrombocytopenia | Yes |
| Nephrotic syndrome | Less common | Proteinuria >3.5g/24h, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia, periorbital edema | Yes |
| Medication-induced | Common | CCBs (dihydropyridines), NSAIDs, thiazolidinediones, corticosteroids | No |
| DVT (unilateral) | Common | Unilateral swelling, pain, warmth, risk factors for VTE | Yes |
| Venous insufficiency | Very common | Dependent edema, worse end of day, skin changes, bilateral or unilateral | No |
| Pulmonary hypertension | Less common | Dyspnea, fatigue, syncope, accentuated P2, RV heave | Yes |
| Lymphedema | Less common | Non-pitting, post-surgical/radiation, filarial | No |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Bilateral edema -> assess for systemic causes: cardiac exam (JVP, S3, crackles), hepatic (ascites, spider angiomata), renal (proteinuria, creatinine)
2. Labs: BMP, LFTs, albumin, CBC, UA with protein, TSH
3. If ascites present -> diagnostic paracentesis: cell count, albumin, protein, culture; calculate SAAG
4. If cardiac suspected -> echocardiogram (EF, diastolic function, pulmonary pressures)
5. If liver disease -> evaluate for cirrhosis (elastography, biopsy); screen for varices
6. Unilateral edema -> Doppler ultrasound for DVT; consider lymphedema, Baker cyst
7. New ascites in cirrhosis or clinical change -> always perform diagnostic paracentesis for SBP

## Clinical Pearls
- Always look for systemic causes in bilateral leg edema before considering local causes
- SBP can be silent in 13% of patients; always paracentesize on admission or clinical change
- Ammonia level cannot determine presence or absence of hepatic encephalopathy
- Hepatorenal syndrome: 6-month mortality ~50% (type 2) to ~100% (type 1)
- Nephrotic syndrome: DVT risk increases with albumin <2.0-2.5 and proteinuria >8 g/24h
- 83% of HFpEF patients have pulmonary hypertension
- Hypothyroidism causes non-pitting edema (myxedema), not typical pitting edema
- Cirrhosis median survival: 10-13 yrs compensated; 5-yr mortality 85% after decompensation

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- New ascites + fever/abdominal pain/mental status change -> rule out SBP -> emergent paracentesis
- Bilateral edema + JVD + S3 + crackles -> decompensated heart failure -> urgent evaluation
- Unilateral painful swollen limb -> DVT -> Doppler ultrasound -> anticoagulation
- Cirrhosis + creatinine >1.5 persisting after albumin challenge -> hepatorenal syndrome
- Variceal bleeding + ascites: 1-year mortality 57%; 6-week mortality of variceal bleed 15-20%
