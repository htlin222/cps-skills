## Differential Diagnosis Framework
Organized anatomically from skin to internal organs: chest wall (musculoskeletal), pleura, lungs, mediastinum, esophagus, heart/pericardium, aorta. Three **must-not-miss** diagnoses dominate: ACS, PE, and acute aortic syndrome (AAS). Pivotal points: acute vs chronic, vital sign abnormalities, character/radiation of pain.

## Key Differential Diagnoses
| Diagnosis | Prevalence (ED) | Key Features | Must-Not-Miss |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------|---------------|
| ACS (MI/UA) | 5-15% | Substernal pressure, radiation to arm/jaw, diaphoresis, exertional | YES |
| Pulmonary embolism | 2-5% | Pleuritic, dyspnea, tachycardia, risk factors (immobility, cancer) | YES |
| Aortic dissection | <1% | Tearing/ripping, sudden onset, BP differential, wide mediastinum | YES |
| GERD | 10-20% | Burning, postprandial, relieved by antacids; 97% specificity with endoscopy | No |
| Musculoskeletal | 20-50% | Reproducible with palpation, positional, follows injury/strain | No |
| Pneumothorax | 1-2% | Sudden pleuritic pain, decreased breath sounds, hyperresonance | YES |
| Pericarditis | 1-5% | Sharp, positional (worse supine, better sitting forward), friction rub | Tamponade |
| Pleural effusion | Variable | Dullness to percussion, decreased breath sounds | No |

## Diagnostic Algorithm
1. **Acute vs. Chronic**: Acute chest pain → prioritize ACS, PE, AAS (the "big 3")
2. **Vital signs**: Hypotension, tachycardia, hypoxia → emergent workup
3. **ECG**: Obtain immediately. ST elevation → cath lab. Any abnormality → LR- 0.04 if normal (MI unlikely)
4. **Troponin**: Serial troponins. LR+ 48 when positive; LR- 0.05 when negative
5. **Risk stratify ACS**: History + ECG + troponin. If low risk → consider stress testing
6. **Consider PE**: Use Wells score → D-dimer if low probability → CTA if elevated
7. **Consider AAS**: Sudden tearing pain + BP differential → CT angiography
8. **If non-cardiac**: GERD (trial PPI + endoscopy, 97% specificity combined), MSK (reproducible tenderness)

## Clinical Pearls
- 65% of positive PE findings have specificity >80%; 43% have specificity >90%
- "Fingerprint" findings (FP) are rarely seen without the disease — like fingerprints pointing to one person
- Do NOT be fooled by the negative: only 21% of negative findings have sensitivity >80%
- Normal ECG makes acute MI very unlikely (LR- 0.04) but does NOT rule out unstable angina
- Pretest probability matters enormously: even with negative stress test (LR- 0.15), a patient with 92% pretest probability still has 60% posttest probability
- GERD can mimic angina: both cause exertional pressure sensation. Suspicious history + endoscopy has 97% specificity

## Red Flags (Must-Not-Miss)
- **ACS**: New ST elevation, new Q waves, rising troponin, hemodynamic instability
- **PE**: Sudden dyspnea + pleuritic pain + tachycardia + risk factors; D-dimer negative makes it very unlikely (LR- 0.05)
- **Aortic dissection**: Sudden tearing pain radiating to back, BP differential between arms, wide mediastinum on CXR
- **Tension pneumothorax**: Absent breath sounds unilaterally + hypotension + tracheal deviation → needle decompression
- **Cardiac tamponade**: Beck's triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds) + pulsus paradoxus
