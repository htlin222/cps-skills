# Round 1: Attending Physician (Internal Medicine)

## Problem Representation
This is a 76-year-old woman with heavy smoking history, HTN, DM, and dyslipidemia presenting with **2 weeks of progressive dyspnea** (SpO2 80% on RA, 85% on NRB) requiring intubation, in the setting of **6-month progressive muscle weakness** (proximal > distal, arms and legs), **bilateral ptosis without diplopia**, **absent reflexes throughout**, **intact sensation**, **normal CK**, **low/absent CMAPs on NCS**, **prior SIADH** (3 months ago), and **failure to respond to MG-directed therapy** (pyridostigmine, IVIG, glucocorticoids).

## Key Pivotal Points
1. **Neuromuscular respiratory failure** (not primary cardiac/pulmonary): Progressive weakness → diaphragmatic weakness → hypercapnic respiratory failure
2. **Motor > sensory**: Weakness with intact sensation → anterior horn cell, NMJ, or muscle
3. **Areflexia**: Rules out UMN/myelopathy; distinguishes from typical MG (reflexes usually preserved)
4. **Ptosis without ophthalmoplegia**: Partial NMJ or neuromuscular involvement
5. **Low/absent CMAPs**: Suggests motor axon loss OR NMJ presynaptic transmission failure
6. **Normal CK**: Against active myositis/myopathy
7. **Failed MG therapy**: Against typical seropositive MG
8. **Prior SIADH**: Paraneoplastic? (attributed to SSRI but may not have been)
9. **Heavy smoker**: High risk for small cell lung cancer (SCLC)
10. **Small pleural effusion on CXR**: Could indicate malignancy

|------|-----------|---------------|--------------|------------------|---------------|
| 1 | **Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS)** | 35% | Proximal weakness, areflexia, ptosis, low CMAPs, heavy smoker (SCLC risk), SIADH (paraneoplastic), failed MG therapy, respiratory failure | Edrophonium showed mild eye improvement | YES |

## Why LEMS Is the Leading Hypothesis

The constellation is highly characteristic of LEMS:

1. **Proximal weakness + areflexia + autonomic dysfunction (SIADH)** = classic LEMS triad
2. **Low/absent CMAPs** = hallmark (impaired presynaptic ACh release → reduced baseline CMAP amplitude)
3. **Heavy smoker** = high risk for SCLC, which causes ~60% of LEMS cases
4. **SIADH** = likely paraneoplastic (ectopic ADH from SCLC), not SSRI-related
5. **Failed standard MG therapy** = LEMS responds poorly to pyridostigmine alone
6. **Mild edrophonium response** = LEMS can show partial response
7. **Small pleural effusion** = could represent underlying malignancy
8. **Ptosis without full ophthalmoplegia** = LEMS causes mild ocular symptoms (unlike MG)

### Key MG vs LEMS Distinction
| Feature | MG | LEMS |
|---------|-----|------|
| Reflexes | Preserved | Absent (post-tetanic potentiation may restore briefly) |
| Edrophonium | Strong response | Weak/partial response |
| Pyridostigmine | Effective | Poorly effective alone |
| CMAPs | Normal amplitude | Low/absent amplitude |
| RNS (low-rate) | Decremental response | Decremental response |
| RNS (high-rate) | No increment | >100% increment (facilitation) |
| Ocular | Prominent (diplopia, ptosis) | Mild (ptosis, no diplopia typical) |
| Autonomic | Rare | Common (dry mouth, SIADH, orthostasis) |

## Recommended Next Steps
1. **Anti-P/Q-type VGCC antibodies** — diagnostic for LEMS (sensitivity ~85-90%)
2. **Repetitive nerve stimulation at 50 Hz** or post-exercise CMAP — look for >100% increment
3. **CT chest with contrast** — search for SCLC
4. **Anti-SOX1 antibodies** — paraneoplastic marker for SCLC-LEMS
5. If CT negative: **PET-CT** for occult malignancy
