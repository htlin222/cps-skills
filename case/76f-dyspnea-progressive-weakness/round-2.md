# Round 2: Diagnostic Testing (Radiology & Pathology)

## Radiologist Assessment

### Available Imaging Interpretation

**CXR — Small pleural effusion:**
A small pleural effusion in a 76-year-old heavy smoker with progressive neuromuscular disease is nonspecific but concerning. The differential for pleural effusion here includes malignancy (SCLC with pleural involvement), heart failure (she has HTN/DM risk factors), or parapneumonic process. However, the absence of consolidation on CXR and the lack of infectious symptoms make infection unlikely. The effusion is small and cannot explain her degree of hypoxemia (SpO2 80%), reinforcing that hypoxemia is neuromuscular in origin (hypoventilation leading to hypercapnic respiratory failure).

Critically, **CXR has poor sensitivity (~50%) for detecting early-stage SCLC**, particularly small hilar or mediastinal masses that can be obscured by mediastinal structures or the cardiac silhouette. A normal-appearing CXR does not rule out SCLC.

**MRI lumbar spine — Mild lumbar stenosis only:**
Mild lumbar stenosis in a 76-year-old is an age-related incidental finding and does not explain proximal-predominant weakness, ptosis, areflexia, or respiratory failure. This rules out compressive myelopathy and cauda equina syndrome as contributors to her presentation.

### Required Additional Imaging

1. **CT chest with IV contrast (URGENT):** First-line imaging to evaluate for SCLC. Must include evaluation of the hilum, mediastinum, and pleural space. SCLC typically presents as a central/hilar mass with mediastinal lymphadenopathy. Approximately 60% of LEMS cases are paraneoplastic, with SCLC responsible for >95% of those. Sensitivity of CT for detecting SCLC is ~90-95%.

2. **PET-CT (18F-FDG):** If CT chest is negative or equivocal, PET-CT is essential. SCLC is highly FDG-avid. PET-CT can detect occult primary tumors as small as 7-10 mm and identify metastatic disease. In paraneoplastic LEMS with negative CT, PET-CT detects the underlying malignancy in an additional ~10-15% of cases.

3. **Diagnostic thoracentesis with cytology:** The pleural effusion, though small, should be sampled if CT suggests malignant etiology. Malignant pleural effusion from SCLC has ~60% cytology yield on first tap.

## Pathologist Assessment

### Laboratory Interpretation

**Routine labs unremarkable:** Rules out metabolic causes of weakness (hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, hypothyroidism, hypercalcemia). Does not clarify the neuromuscular diagnosis.

**CK normal:** This is a critical negative finding.
- LR- for inflammatory myopathy: ~0.1 (elevated CK expected in >95% of polymyositis/dermatomyositis)
- Rules out active muscle destruction — strongly against polymyositis, dermatomyositis, necrotizing autoimmune myopathy
- Compatible with NMJ disorders (LEMS, MG) and neuropathies, where CK is typically normal

**CSF — No cells, normal protein and glucose, cytology negative:**
- Normal protein (LR- ~0.3 for GBS/CIDP, where albuminocytologic dissociation expected in ~80%)
- Negative cytology rules out leptomeningeal carcinomatosis (sensitivity ~50-60% on single tap, but low pre-test probability here)
- Normal CSF is compatible with NMJ disorders (LEMS, MG) where CSF is expected to be normal

### Electrodiagnostic Interpretation

**NCS — Low-amplitude or absent CMAPs in arms and legs:**
This is the most diagnostically powerful finding. Low/absent CMAPs with intact sensation creates a specific pattern:

- In LEMS: Low CMAPs result from impaired presynaptic calcium channel function leading to reduced ACh release and reduced baseline CMAP amplitude. This is the electrophysiologic hallmark. LR+ for LEMS when combined with the clinical picture: ~8-10.
- In motor neuron disease: Low CMAPs from motor axon degeneration, but would expect fibrillation potentials on EMG (not yet performed).
- In severe motor neuropathy: Possible but unusual pattern without sensory involvement.

**RNS — Inconclusive:**
This is unfortunate. In LEMS, low-frequency (2-3 Hz) RNS produces decremental response (also seen in MG), but **high-frequency (50 Hz) RNS or post-exercise facilitation produces >100% CMAP increment** — this is pathognomonic for LEMS (LR+ ~25-30 for LEMS vs other NMJ disorders). The inconclusive result may reflect technical limitations in an edematous, weak patient. Repeat RNS with post-exercise protocol should be attempted.

**Edrophonium test — Mild eye improvement only:**
- Strong positive edrophonium response: LR+ ~10 for MG
- Mild/equivocal response: LR+ only ~1.5-2 for MG; does NOT rule out LEMS
- LEMS can show partial edrophonium response because the drug increases ACh persistence at the NMJ, partially compensating for reduced release
- The weak response argues against seropositive MG (where robust improvement is expected)

### Required Additional Laboratory Testing

1. **Anti-P/Q-type VGCC antibodies (URGENT):** Sensitivity 85-90% for LEMS, specificity >95%. This is the confirmatory serologic test. LR+ ~17-20, LR- ~0.1-0.15.

2. **Anti-SOX1 antibodies:** Present in ~65% of SCLC-associated LEMS vs ~5% of non-tumor LEMS. Highly specific paraneoplastic marker. If positive, mandates aggressive tumor search even if initial imaging is negative.

3. **Anti-AChR and anti-MuSK antibodies:** Should be sent to formally exclude seropositive MG and MuSK-MG (though clinical picture is against both).

4. **Repeat RNS with post-exercise facilitation protocol:** Look for >100% CMAP increment post-exercise or at 50 Hz stimulation.

5. **Anti-Hu (ANNA-1) antibodies:** To evaluate for paraneoplastic motor neuron disease or sensorimotor neuropathy associated with SCLC.

## Recommendations

### Immediate (within 24 hours)
1. **CT chest with IV contrast** — search for SCLC (central mass, hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy)
2. **Anti-P/Q-type VGCC antibodies** — confirmatory for LEMS
3. **Repeat RNS with post-exercise facilitation** — look for >100% CMAP increment
4. **Anti-SOX1, anti-Hu (ANNA-1) antibodies** — paraneoplastic markers

### If VGCC antibodies positive
- Confirms LEMS diagnosis
- Begin 3,4-diaminopyridine (amifampridine) — first-line LEMS therapy
- Aggressive cancer screening: CT chest, then PET-CT, then bronchoscopy if mass identified
- Consider plasma exchange for acute stabilization while awaiting tumor treatment

### If CT chest shows mass
- Urgent tissue biopsy (bronchoscopic or CT-guided)
- Staging with PET-CT and brain MRI
- Oncology consultation for SCLC-directed therapy (chemoradiation often improves LEMS)

### If initial workup negative
- PET-CT for occult malignancy
- Anti-MuSK and anti-AChR antibodies to formally exclude MG variants
- Acid maltase (GAA enzyme) assay if Pompe still considered
- Repeat cancer screening every 3-6 months for 2 years (SCLC can appear after LEMS diagnosis)
