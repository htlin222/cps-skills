# Round 3: Subspecialty Consultation (Neurology)

## Neuromuscular Localization

This case localizes to the **presynaptic neuromuscular junction**, with the following reasoning:

**Why NMJ (not muscle):**
- Normal CK excludes active myopathy, inflammatory myositis, or muscular dystrophy
- Ptosis is a classic NMJ feature; primary myopathies rarely cause ptosis (except rare exceptions like oculopharyngeal muscular dystrophy, which presents differently)
- Proximal > distal gradient is consistent with NMJ disease

**Why presynaptic NMJ (not postsynaptic):**
- **Areflexia** -- In postsynaptic NMJ disease (MG), reflexes are preserved because the motor nerve and presynaptic terminal function normally. In presynaptic disease (LEMS), insufficient acetylcholine (ACh) release at both the NMJ and the monosynaptic reflex arc results in absent reflexes
- **Low/absent CMAPs** -- This is the electrodiagnostic hallmark of presynaptic failure. In MG, baseline CMAPs are normal because the presynaptic terminal releases adequate quanta; the problem is postsynaptic receptor loss. In LEMS, impaired P/Q-type VGCC function reduces quantal release, producing low baseline CMAPs
- **Poor response to AChE inhibitors** -- Pyridostigmine prolongs ACh action at the synapse. When the problem is insufficient ACh release (presynaptic), increasing its dwell time provides minimal benefit. When the problem is receptor blockade/loss (postsynaptic MG), it helps substantially
- **Mild edrophonium response limited to eyes only** -- Partial improvement can occur in LEMS because some ACh release still occurs, but the improvement is characteristically incomplete and limited compared to the dramatic response seen in MG

**Why not motor neuron disease:**
- Ptosis is distinctly unusual in ALS/motor neuron disease (extraocular and eyelid muscles are typically spared)
- No fasciculations documented
- No upper motor neuron signs (no hyperreflexia, no Babinski, no spasticity)
- Pattern of weakness (proximal >> distal initially) is more NMJ than anterior horn cell
- SIADH is not a feature of ALS but is well-recognized in paraneoplastic syndromes

## NCS/EMG Interpretation

### Low/Absent CMAPs: The Critical Finding

The nerve conduction study showing **low-amplitude or absent CMAPs in both arms and legs** is the single most important electrodiagnostic finding in this case. This finding dramatically narrows the differential:

| Condition | Expected CMAP Amplitude | Mechanism |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------|
| **LEMS** | **Low/absent** | Impaired presynaptic ACh vesicle release due to anti-VGCC antibodies |
| MG | Normal | Postsynaptic receptor loss; presynaptic release is intact |
| ALS | Low (in denervated muscles) | Motor neuron death leads to axon loss, but patchy, not diffuse |
| CIDP | Low (if demyelinating) | Conduction block and temporal dispersion |
| Botulism | Low/absent | Presynaptic toxin blocks vesicle fusion |

In LEMS, CMAPs are diffusely reduced across multiple nerves and territories because the autoimmune attack targets P/Q-type VGCCs ubiquitously. This differentiates it from ALS (where CMAP reduction is patchy, corresponding to affected motor units) and from CIDP (where conduction block, prolonged distal latencies, and slow conduction velocities are expected).

### Why RNS Was Inconclusive

Repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS) was reported as inconclusive. This is a common problem in clinical practice and likely occurred because:

1. **Only low-frequency (2-3 Hz) RNS was performed** -- At low rates, both LEMS and MG show decremental responses (>10% decrement), making them indistinguishable. The test that differentiates LEMS is high-frequency stimulation or post-exercise testing
2. **Severely reduced baseline CMAPs** -- When CMAPs are already very low or absent, measuring a percentage decrement becomes technically unreliable
3. **Patient cooperation** -- The patient is intubated and profoundly weak, making voluntary muscle activation for post-exercise studies difficult

### What Should Be Done Instead

The definitive electrodiagnostic test for LEMS is:

- **High-frequency RNS (20-50 Hz)** or, more commonly, **post-exercise facilitation testing**: Record CMAP at rest, then either stimulate at 50 Hz for 10 seconds or have the patient maximally contract the muscle for 10-15 seconds, then immediately re-record
- **Diagnostic criterion**: >100% increment (doubling) of CMAP amplitude after exercise or high-frequency stimulation. This is pathognomonic for LEMS
- **Mechanism**: Rapid repetitive stimulation causes calcium accumulation in the presynaptic terminal, temporarily overcoming the antibody-mediated VGCC blockade, releasing more ACh vesicles and producing a larger CMAP
- Since this patient cannot voluntarily contract muscles adequately, **high-frequency RNS at 50 Hz** is the preferred approach in this ICU setting

In MG, high-frequency stimulation does NOT produce facilitation (and may worsen the decrement). In LEMS, facilitation >100% has a **sensitivity of approximately 95% and specificity >95%** for the diagnosis.

## LEMS-Specific Analysis

### Classic Triad Present

LEMS is defined by three cardinal features, all present in this patient:

1. **Proximal muscle weakness** -- Present for 6 months, legs > arms, progressive. This is the presenting symptom in >90% of LEMS patients
2. **Areflexia/hyporeflexia** -- Absent throughout. Present in approximately 90% of LEMS cases. Reflexes may transiently return after sustained muscle contraction (post-tetanic potentiation) -- this is a bedside test worth attempting
3. **Autonomic dysfunction** -- SIADH 3 months ago. While attributed to SSRI, SIADH in LEMS can result from either (a) paraneoplastic ectopic ADH secretion by SCLC, or (b) direct autonomic dysfunction from anti-VGCC antibodies affecting autonomic ganglia. Other autonomic features to assess: dry mouth (present in approximately 75% of LEMS), constipation, erectile dysfunction, orthostatic hypotension

### Additional LEMS-Consistent Features

- **Ptosis without diplopia/ophthalmoplegia**: Ocular involvement in LEMS is characteristically milder than MG. Approximately 70% of LEMS patients develop ptosis, but severe ophthalmoplegia is uncommon
- **Respiratory failure**: Occurs in severe LEMS, particularly when untreated or when underlying malignancy is advancing
- **Failed MG-directed therapy**: LEMS responds poorly to pyridostigmine alone (unlike MG). The treatment for LEMS is 3,4-diaminopyridine (3,4-DAP), which enhances presynaptic ACh release by blocking potassium channels and prolonging the presynaptic action potential

### Paraneoplastic Context

- **60% of LEMS is paraneoplastic**, predominantly associated with SCLC
- This patient's heavy smoking history, SIADH, and pleural effusion create a high pretest probability for SCLC
- SCLC expresses P/Q-type VGCCs on its cell surface, triggering the immune response that cross-reacts with neuronal VGCCs
- The DELTA-P score (Dutch-English LEMS Tumor Association Prediction) can estimate cancer probability in LEMS: scoring includes weight loss, smoking, age at onset, bulbar involvement, and male sex

## Differential Exclusion

### Why This Is NOT Myasthenia Gravis

| Feature | Expected in MG | This Patient | Verdict |
|---------|---------------|--------------|---------|
| Reflexes | Normal/preserved | Absent throughout | **Against MG** |
| Baseline CMAPs | Normal amplitude | Low/absent | **Against MG** |
| Edrophonium response | Dramatic improvement | Mild eye improvement only | **Against MG** |
| Pyridostigmine response | Clinical improvement | No improvement | **Against MG** |
| IVIG response | Often improves within 2-4 weeks | No improvement | **Against MG** |
| Diplopia | Common (approximately 85%) | Absent | Somewhat against MG |
| Ophthalmoplegia | Common | Absent | Somewhat against MG |

Anti-MuSK MG can cause respiratory failure with poor IVIG response, but even MuSK-MG preserves reflexes and has normal baseline CMAPs. The combination of areflexia + low CMAPs + poor AChE inhibitor response essentially excludes all forms of MG.

### Why This Is NOT ALS

- **Ptosis** -- ALS spares eyelid and extraocular muscles; ptosis is distinctly unusual and should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis
- **No UMN signs** -- ALS (except PMA variant) requires both UMN and LMN signs
- **No fasciculations** -- While not always present, fasciculations are common in ALS
- **SIADH** -- Not a recognized feature of ALS
- **Diffusely low CMAPs without fibrillation potentials on NCS** -- ALS produces patchy denervation with fibrillation potentials; EMG was not performed but NCS alone shows diffuse, symmetric CMAP reduction more consistent with NMJ disease
- **Temporal profile** -- 6 months of proximal weakness progressing to respiratory failure is rapid for ALS, where respiratory failure typically occurs later in the disease course

### Why This Is NOT CIDP

- **Normal CSF protein** -- CIDP characteristically shows elevated CSF protein (cytoalbuminous dissociation) in >80% of cases. Normal protein argues strongly against CIDP (LR- approximately 0.1)
- **Ptosis** -- Not a feature of CIDP
- **Failed IVIG** -- CIDP typically responds to IVIG
- **No sensory findings** -- CIDP usually has sensory involvement

### Why This Is NOT Late-Onset Pompe Disease

- **Ptosis** -- Not a feature of Pompe disease
- **Areflexia** -- Unusual in Pompe
- **Normal CK** -- Pompe usually shows elevated CK (2-10x normal)
- **Low CMAPs** -- Not a feature of Pompe (NCS is typically normal or shows myopathic changes on EMG)

### Why This Is NOT Botulism

- **6-month progressive course** -- Botulism causes acute descending paralysis over hours to days, not months
- Otherwise the electrodiagnostic profile (low CMAPs with facilitation) can resemble LEMS

## Recommendations

### Immediate Diagnostic Workup (within 24-48 hours)

1. **Anti-P/Q-type VGCC antibodies** -- Sensitivity approximately 85-90% for LEMS. This is the confirmatory serologic test. Send to a reference lab (Mayo, Quest, ARUP). Turnaround 5-7 days, but clinical suspicion is sufficient to initiate treatment
2. **High-frequency RNS (50 Hz) with CMAP facilitation measurement** -- Look for >100% increment. This can confirm the diagnosis at the bedside today. Since the patient cannot voluntarily contract, use 50 Hz stimulation directly. Record from abductor digiti minimi or abductor pollicis brevis
3. **CT chest with IV contrast** -- Urgent. Looking for SCLC, hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy. If negative, proceed to PET-CT
4. **Paraneoplastic antibody panel**: Anti-SOX1 (present in approximately 65% of SCLC-LEMS, highly specific), anti-Hu/ANNA-1 (associated with SCLC sensorimotor neuropathy), anti-CRMP5/CV2

### Additional Bedside Assessment

5. **Post-tetanic potentiation of reflexes** -- Have the patient (or passively) contract the quadriceps for 10-15 seconds, then immediately test the patellar reflex. Transient return of the reflex is nearly pathognomonic for LEMS
6. **Ice test on ptosis** -- While less specific, improvement with cooling favors NMJ disease generally
7. **Autonomic review** -- Document dry mouth, constipation, orthostatic BP measurements, pupillary responses

### Immediate Treatment (do not wait for antibody results)

8. **3,4-Diaminopyridine (amifampridine)** -- First-line symptomatic treatment for LEMS. Blocks presynaptic potassium channels, prolongs action potential, increases calcium entry, increases ACh release. Start 5 mg TID, titrate to 20 mg QID. This drug is specific to LEMS and would not help in MG
9. **Continue IVIG** (or switch to plasma exchange) -- While IVIG failed for MG, the dose/duration may have been insufficient for LEMS. Alternatively, plasmapheresis may be more effective in the acute setting
10. **If SCLC confirmed**: Oncology consultation for urgent chemotherapy -- Tumor-directed therapy often produces the most dramatic improvement in paraneoplastic LEMS
11. **Maintain ventilator support** -- Respiratory failure in LEMS can be reversible with treatment; do not consider withdrawal prematurely

### If Anti-VGCC Antibodies Return Negative (approximately 10-15% of LEMS)

12. Consider **anti-ganglionic AChR antibodies** (autoimmune autonomic ganglionopathy overlap)
13. Repeat NCS with high-frequency RNS if not yet done -- electrodiagnostic confirmation can establish the diagnosis even in seronegative cases
14. If CT chest negative, obtain **PET-CT** -- SCLC can be occult and may appear up to 2 years after LEMS diagnosis. Current guidelines recommend surveillance imaging every 6 months for at least 2 years
