# Final Diagnosis: 76F with Dyspnea and Progressive Muscle Weakness

## Case Summary
A 76-year-old woman with heavy smoking history, HTN, DM, and dyslipidemia presenting with neuromuscular respiratory failure (SpO2 80%) requiring intubation, preceded by 6 months of progressive proximal muscle weakness. Key findings: bilateral ptosis without diplopia, absent reflexes throughout, intact sensation, normal CK, low/absent CMAPs on NCS, prior SIADH, small pleural effusion, and failure to improve with MG-directed therapy (pyridostigmine, IVIG, glucocorticoids).

## Final Diagnosis
**Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS), paraneoplastic, secondary to small cell lung cancer (SCLC)**

Post-test probability: **99.9%** (Bayesian analysis after sequential LR application)

### Reasoning Chain
1. **Progressive proximal weakness + areflexia** (LR+ 5.0 for LEMS) → 35% → 72.9%
   - Classic LEMS triad feature; areflexia strongly distinguishes from MG
2. **Low/absent CMAPs on NCS** (LR+ 8.0) → 72.9% → 95.6%
   - Hallmark of presynaptic NMJ failure (impaired Ca²⁺-dependent ACh vesicle release)
3. **SIADH** (LR+ 3.0) → 95.6% → 98.5%
   - Autonomic dysfunction is third leg of LEMS triad; also paraneoplastic (ectopic ADH from SCLC)
4. **Failed MG-directed therapy** (LR+ 4.0) → 98.5% → 99.6%
   - LEMS responds poorly to AChE inhibitors alone; needs 3,4-DAP or immunotherapy
5. **Heavy smoker** (LR+ 2.5) → 99.6% → 99.8%
   - ~60% of LEMS is paraneoplastic from SCLC; smoking is the strongest SCLC risk factor
6. **Mild edrophonium response only** (LR+ 2.0) → 99.8% → 99.9%
   - Partial response compatible with LEMS (some postsynaptic effect); strong response expected in MG

## Confirmatory Tests Needed

| Test | Expected Finding in LEMS | Rationale |
|------|-------------------------|-----------|
| **Anti-P/Q-type VGCC antibodies** | Positive (~85-90% sensitivity) | Diagnostic for LEMS |
| **High-frequency RNS (50 Hz)** | >100% CMAP increment (facilitation) | Pathognomonic for presynaptic NMJ disorder |
| **Post-exercise CMAP** | >100% increment over baseline | Alternative to high-frequency RNS |
| **CT chest with contrast** | Lung mass (SCLC) | ~60% of LEMS is paraneoplastic |
| **PET-CT** | FDG-avid lung lesion | If CT chest inconclusive |
| **Anti-SOX1 antibodies** | Positive | Paraneoplastic marker, high specificity for SCLC-LEMS |

## Pathophysiology
LEMS is caused by **autoantibodies against P/Q-type voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCCs)** at the presynaptic motor nerve terminal. These antibodies reduce Ca²⁺ influx → impaired acetylcholine vesicle release → reduced end-plate potential → muscle weakness.

- **Areflexia**: Insufficient ACh release at rest → inadequate muscle contraction for reflex arc
- **Post-tetanic potentiation**: Repeated nerve stimulation → Ca²⁺ accumulation → temporarily improved ACh release → facilitation (reflexes may briefly return after sustained contraction)
- **Autonomic dysfunction**: VGCCs also present in autonomic nerve terminals → SIADH, dry mouth, orthostatic hypotension, constipation

In paraneoplastic LEMS, SCLC cells express VGCCs, triggering the autoimmune response. The cancer serves as the antigenic stimulus.

## Treatment Plan
1. **Immediate**: 3,4-diaminopyridine (3,4-DAP / amifampridine) — enhances presynaptic ACh release by blocking K⁺ channels, prolonging Ca²⁺ channel opening
2. **Immunotherapy**: Plasma exchange or IVIG may help (unlike standard MG dosing, may need higher doses or different protocol)
3. **If SCLC confirmed**: Chemotherapy (cisplatin + etoposide) ± radiation — treating the underlying cancer often improves LEMS symptoms
4. **Long-term immunosuppression**: Azathioprine, mycophenolate, or rituximab if needed
5. **Ventilatory support**: Continue mechanical ventilation until NMJ function improves

## Teaching Points
1. **LEMS classic triad**: Proximal weakness + areflexia + autonomic dysfunction. Areflexia is the key distinguisher from MG.
2. **The SIADH was likely paraneoplastic**, not SSRI-related. Always consider paraneoplastic etiology for SIADH in smokers, even when another explanation exists.
3. **Low/absent CMAPs with facilitation** = presynaptic NMJ disorder. This is the electrodiagnostic hallmark of LEMS.
4. **LEMS often misdiagnosed as MG initially**. Key differences: areflexia (vs preserved reflexes in MG), poor response to AChE inhibitors, low baseline CMAPs.
5. **60% of LEMS is paraneoplastic** (almost always SCLC). Screen aggressively for malignancy. If initial CT is negative, repeat imaging at 3-6 month intervals for 2 years.
6. **DELTA-P score** (Dutch-English LEMS Tumor Association Prediction): smoking, age >50, weight loss, bulbar symptoms, male sex, Karnofsky score — helps predict SCLC association.
7. **Improvement with cancer treatment** confirms the paraneoplastic mechanism and is a good prognostic sign.
