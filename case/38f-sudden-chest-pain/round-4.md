## SCAD Evidence Summary

### Epidemiology
- **SCAD is the leading non-atherosclerotic cause of MI in young women.** It accounts for up to 35% of MIs in women under 50 and an estimated 7.6% specifically in women under 40.
- SCAD represents ~4% of all ACS presentations overall but is disproportionately concentrated in younger female populations.
- 80-95% of SCAD patients are women, with a mean age of ~43 years.
- SCAD was identified in 0.78% of 26,598 consecutive MI hospitalizations, but this is likely underestimated due to historical under-recognition.

### Recurrence Rates
- **Recurrence is a major concern in SCAD**, unlike atherosclerotic MI where secondary prevention is well-established.
- Recurrent MI occurs in ~16.8% at median 3.1-year follow-up.
- Peak recurrence window: first 4-6 months after index event.
- 10-year MACE rate: estimated at 47% (including death, heart failure, recurrent MI, SCAD recurrence).
- **Risk factors for recurrence**: hypertension, FMD, migraine.
- **Protective factor**: beta-blocker use was associated with reduced recurrence risk.
- Pregnancy-associated SCAD carries recurrence rates of 10-29%.

### Relevance to This Case
A 38-year-old woman with prior MI and recurrent acute chest pain fits the classic SCAD profile: young female, physically active (mail carrier), history of prior MI (possibly prior SCAD), and now presenting with a recurrent event. The recurrence rate data (16.8% at 3 years) supports SCAD recurrence as a leading hypothesis.

## Key Diagnostic Evidence

### Nitroglycerin Non-Response
- **NTG response is NOT a reliable discriminator** between cardiac and non-cardiac chest pain (sensitivity 72%, specificity 37%).
- In a landmark study, NTG relieved pain in only 35% of patients with active CAD, while also relieving pain in 41% of patients without CAD.
- **Clinical implication**: NTG non-response in this patient does NOT argue against ACS. It is consistent with several etiologies including SCAD (where the mechanism is intramural hematoma/dissection rather than vasospasm), fixed coronary occlusion, aortic dissection, pulmonary embolism, or pericarditis.
- Current AHA/ACC guidelines state NTG response should not be used as a diagnostic criterion.

### Diagnostic Approach for Suspected SCAD
1. **Coronary angiography** remains the first-line diagnostic modality.
   - Type 1 SCAD: visible intimal flap with contrast staining (pathognomonic)
   - Type 2 SCAD: diffuse long stenosis (most common, ~67% of cases)
   - Type 3 SCAD: focal stenosis mimicking atherosclerosis (most challenging to diagnose)

2. **Intravascular imaging (OCT/IVUS)** -- Class IIb recommendation per AHA:
   - OCT: superior spatial resolution, better for identifying intimal tears
   - IVUS: better depth penetration for larger vessels; does NOT require contrast (advantage in SCAD as contrast injection risks hematoma propagation)
   - **Should NOT be performed routinely** if SCAD diagnosis is clear on angiography and conservative management is planned (risk of extending dissection)
   - Reserved for ambiguous angiographic findings, particularly to distinguish Type 3 SCAD from atherosclerosis

3. **Screening for FMD**: Given >50% co-prevalence, patients diagnosed with SCAD should undergo vascular screening (renal, cerebrovascular) for FMD.

## Treatment Considerations

### Conservative vs. Interventional Management

**Strong evidence favors conservative management for SCAD:**

- **>95% of SCAD lesions spontaneously heal** when assessed by repeat angiography at 30+ days.
- PCI in SCAD has a **high failure rate**: only 34.7% fully successful, 37.3% partially successful, 28.0% unsuccessful.
- PCI complications specific to SCAD: hematoma extension, iatrogenic dissection, need for multiple long stents (increasing restenosis/thrombosis risk).
- Recent national cohort data confirms **optimal medical therapy (OMT) is associated with lower death and complication rates** than PCI.

**PCI should be reserved for:**
- Hemodynamic instability
- Ongoing ischemia with compromised distal flow
- Left main dissection
- Cardiogenic shock

### Medical Therapy for SCAD
- **Aspirin**: standard post-ACS care
- **Beta-blockers**: associated with reduced SCAD recurrence (unique evidence-based benefit)
- **ACE inhibitors/ARBs**: for LV dysfunction if present
- **Statins**: less clear benefit (no atherosclerotic plaque to stabilize); some guidelines recommend if concomitant dyslipidemia
- **Dual antiplatelet therapy**: controversial; used if stented, but risk of hematoma extension in conservatively managed patients
- **Anticoagulation**: generally avoided (risk of hematoma expansion)
- **NTG**: limited benefit given mechanism of obstruction is intramural hematoma, not vasospasm

### MINOCA Consideration
- Women have ~5x higher incidence of MINOCA than men.
- If angiography shows non-obstructive coronaries, cardiac MRI should be performed to differentiate myocarditis, takotsubo, and microvascular dysfunction from SCAD.

## Summary

### Evidence-Based Assessment for This Case (38F, prior MI, recurrent chest pain, NTG non-response)

**Most likely diagnosis: Recurrent SCAD** (supported by epidemiology, demographics, and clinical pattern)

| Factor | Evidence Support |
|--------|-----------------|
| Age 38, female | SCAD accounts for up to 35% of MI in women <50 |
| Prior MI at young age | SCAD recurrence rate 16.8% at 3 years; 47% MACE at 10 years |
| Exertional onset | Physical exertion is a recognized SCAD trigger |
| NTG non-response | Consistent with SCAD mechanism (hematoma, not vasospasm); NTG response is not diagnostically discriminating regardless |
| Physically active occupation | Not protective against SCAD (unlike atherosclerotic CAD) |

**Recommended diagnostic workup:**
1. Emergent coronary angiography with SCAD-aware interpretation
2. Intracoronary imaging (OCT preferred) only if angiography is ambiguous
3. Echocardiography for LV function assessment
4. If SCAD confirmed: vascular screening for FMD (renal duplex, CTA head-to-pelvis)
5. Cardiac MRI if MINOCA pattern on angiography

**Key management principles:**
1. Conservative management preferred unless hemodynamically unstable
2. Beta-blocker therapy (evidence for recurrence reduction)
3. Avoid anticoagulation (hematoma extension risk)
4. Cautious use of NTG (unlikely to benefit SCAD mechanism)
5. Long-term surveillance for recurrence (highest risk in first 4-6 months)
6. Screen for associated conditions: FMD (>50%), connective tissue disorders, migraine
