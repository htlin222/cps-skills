# Round 3: Subspecialty Consultation (Cardiology)

## SCAD Analysis

The single most important diagnostic consideration in this patient is **spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD)**.

**Epidemiologic rationale:**
- SCAD accounts for **25-35% of MI in women under 50** and is the **#1 cause of MI in young women**
- Median age at presentation: 44-53 years; this patient at 38 is within the typical range
- >90% of SCAD patients are female
- Prior MI in a woman <40 is exceedingly rare from atherosclerotic disease alone (prevalence of MI in women 35-44: ~0.5-1%); SCAD should be considered the etiology of her prior MI until proven otherwise

**Risk factors present in this patient:**
- Female sex (strongest risk factor)
- Physical exertion as trigger (mail carrier walking home) — physical and emotional stress precipitate 40-50% of SCAD events
- Prior MI — SCAD has a **recurrence rate of 10-30% over 10 years** (some registries report up to 17% at 4 years). This presentation may represent recurrent SCAD

**Associated conditions to evaluate:**
- **Fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD)**: present in 40-70% of SCAD patients; requires screening with CTA of renal and cerebrovascular arteries
- Connective tissue disorders (Ehlers-Danlos type IV, Marfan syndrome)
- Peripartum status (pregnancy-associated SCAD is the most severe subtype)
- Systemic inflammatory conditions

## Coronary Vasospasm vs Mechanical Obstruction

The **failure of 4 sublingual nitroglycerin tablets** is a critical discriminating feature.

**Against coronary vasospasm (Prinzmetal angina):**
- NTG is first-line therapy for coronary vasospasm and provides relief in >90% of episodes
- NTG non-response has a **negative LR of ~0.1** for vasospasm — effectively ruling it out as the sole mechanism
- Vasospasm typically presents at rest, not with exertion (this patient was walking)
- Exertional triggers favor mechanical obstruction or dissection over vasospasm

**Favoring mechanical obstruction (SCAD or thrombotic occlusion):**
- NTG non-response is expected in SCAD because the lumen compromise is from intramural hematoma, not smooth muscle spasm
- NTG non-response is also consistent with fixed atherosclerotic plaque rupture with thrombosis, but this is far less likely in a 38-year-old woman without known traditional risk factors
- Sudden onset with exertion is consistent with coronary dissection triggered by hemodynamic stress

**Cocaine-induced vasospasm:**
- Must be excluded by urine toxicology, but would typically respond at least partially to NTG + benzodiazepines
- Less likely given the occupational context (mail carrier mid-shift)

## Evaluation Strategy

### Immediate (Emergency Department)

| Test | Rationale | Expected Finding |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| 12-lead ECG | ST-segment changes, T-wave inversions; localize territory | ST elevation or dynamic ST-T changes in a coronary distribution |
| High-sensitivity troponin (serial) | Confirm myocardial injury; trend for peak | Elevated and rising if acute MI |
| CBC, BMP, coagulation panel | Baseline; rule out anemia, renal dysfunction | Baseline for catheterization planning |
| Lipid panel | Evaluate for familial hypercholesterolemia | If LDL >190 mg/dL, consider FH |
| Urine toxicology | Exclude cocaine, amphetamines | Expected negative |
| BNP/NT-proBNP | Assess for heart failure from prior MI | May be elevated if prior LV dysfunction |
| Echocardiography (bedside) | Wall motion abnormalities; LV function from prior MI | Regional wall motion abnormality in prior MI territory +/- new territory |

### Urgent: Coronary Angiography

**Coronary angiography is the gold standard** but carries specific caveats for SCAD:

- **Type 1 SCAD** (multiple radiolucent lumen): identifiable on standard angiography
- **Type 2 SCAD** (diffuse smooth narrowing): can be mistaken for atherosclerosis or normal variant — **most common type, most commonly missed**
- **Type 3 SCAD** (focal stenosis mimicking atherosclerosis): requires **intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) or optical coherence tomography (OCT)** to confirm intramural hematoma

**Critical instruction to the interventional cardiologist:** If angiography does not show a clear culprit atherosclerotic lesion, SCAD must be actively considered. IVUS or OCT should be available and used if suspicion remains.

**PCI caution in SCAD:** Stenting of SCAD lesions carries a **higher complication rate** than in atherosclerotic disease (wire passage can extend the dissection). Conservative management is preferred when possible in SCAD, unless there is ongoing ischemia with left main or proximal LAD involvement.

### Subacute Workup

- **CTA of renal and iliac arteries** — screen for FMD (beaded appearance)
- **CTA or MRA of head and neck** — screen for cerebrovascular FMD and intracranial aneurysms
- **Antiphospholipid antibody panel** (lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I) — thrombotic MI in young women
- **Thrombophilia screen** if no SCAD found on angiography
- **Genetic testing** for connective tissue disorders if clinical suspicion (joint hypermobility, skin findings)
- **Review prior MI records** — was the prior event also SCAD? This would confirm recurrence and change long-term management

### ECG Interpretation in Young Women

- Young women may have baseline ST-segment and T-wave variants that differ from older populations
- **Early repolarization** is less common in women than men but must be considered
- Prior MI may have left Q waves or persistent T-wave inversions that complicate interpretation of new changes
- **Comparison with prior ECG is essential** — new ST changes in the context of old changes require careful territorial analysis

## Likelihood Ratios Applied

| Finding | Condition | LR+ | LR- | Application |
|---------|-----------|-----|-----|-------------|
| Female sex + MI <50 | SCAD (vs atherosclerotic MI) | ~4.0 | 0.3 | Strongly favors SCAD over typical ACS |
| NTG non-response | Mechanical obstruction (vs vasospasm) | ~3.0 | 0.1 | Effectively excludes pure vasospasm |
| Exertional trigger | SCAD | 1.5-2.0 | 0.7 | Mildly increases SCAD probability |
| Prior MI in young woman | SCAD recurrence | ~3.5 | — | Prior MI may itself have been SCAD; recurrence rate 10-30% |
| Physical stress as precipitant | SCAD (vs Prinzmetal) | ~2.5 | 0.5 | Vasospasm typically occurs at rest |

**Standard ACS risk scores (HEART, TIMI) in this population:**
- These scores were derived from populations with predominantly atherosclerotic disease in older adults
- They **underestimate risk in young women with SCAD** because age <45 and female sex score low on TIMI, and "typical" risk factors may be absent
- A low HEART or TIMI score should **NOT provide reassurance** in a young woman with prior MI and current chest pain
- Clinical gestalt and direct coronary evaluation override risk score calculations here

## Recommendations

### Immediate Actions
1. **Activate cardiac catheterization lab** — this patient needs urgent coronary angiography, with IVUS/OCT available
2. **Heparin and dual antiplatelet therapy per standard ACS protocol** pending angiography (note: if SCAD confirmed, anticoagulation strategy may differ)
3. **Continuous telemetry** — SCAD can cause arrhythmia from ischemia
4. **IV beta-blocker** if hypertensive/tachycardic — reduces coronary shear stress (beneficial in SCAD)
5. **Avoid IV NTG escalation** — already demonstrated non-response; NTG will not resolve mechanical obstruction

### At Catheterization
6. **If no clear atherosclerotic culprit found, actively look for SCAD** (especially Type 2, diffuse narrowing)
7. **Use IVUS or OCT liberally** if angiography is ambiguous
8. **Conservative management preferred** if SCAD confirmed with adequate flow — avoid stenting unless ongoing ischemia with hemodynamic compromise or left main/proximal LAD involvement
9. **If PCI required for SCAD**, use bioresorbable scaffolds or drug-eluting stents with extreme caution; minimize wire manipulation

### Post-Catheterization
10. **Screen for FMD** with CTA renal/iliac and CTA head/neck — this changes lifelong surveillance
11. **Antiphospholipid antibody panel** — two positive tests 12 weeks apart confirm diagnosis
12. **Cardiac MRI** if diagnosis remains uncertain — differentiates myocarditis, Takotsubo, and can identify residual intramural hematoma
13. **Obtain prior MI records** — if the prior event was also in a young woman without risk factors, prior SCAD is the presumptive diagnosis
14. **Genetic counseling** if connective tissue disorder suspected
15. **Long-term management if SCAD confirmed**: beta-blocker (first-line for SCAD prevention), cardiac rehabilitation, avoidance of extreme physical exertion, pregnancy counseling if applicable, lifelong cardiology follow-up with annual imaging surveillance
