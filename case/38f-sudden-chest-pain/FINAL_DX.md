# Final Diagnosis: 38F Mail Carrier with Sudden Chest Pain

## Case Summary
A 38-year-old female mail carrier with prior MI (inferior, 2 years ago; anterior, 4 months ago — both from thrombosed coronary aneurysms) presents with acute anterior STEMI from thrombotic occlusion of an aneurysmal LAD. Catheterization reveals diffuse coronary artery aneurysms in all three vessels. No atherosclerotic risk factors (normal lipids, no DM, never smoker, no cocaine). Dermatology exam reveals ≥6 café-au-lait spots, axillary freckling, and multiple neurofibromas. Family history: father died SCA at 45, grandfather died heart disease at 37.

## Final Diagnosis
**Neurofibromatosis Type 1 (NF1) Vasculopathy with Diffuse Coronary Artery Aneurysms and Recurrent Thrombotic Myocardial Infarction**

## Diagnostic Criteria Met (NIH Consensus — need ≥2 of 7)
1. ✅ ≥6 café-au-lait macules >15mm
2. ✅ ≥2 neurofibromas (multiple soft papules)
3. ✅ Axillary freckling (Crowe sign)
4. Probable: first-degree relative with NF1 (father's premature SCA)

## Diagnostic Evolution — A Lesson in Bayesian Humility

| Phase | Leading Dx | Prob | What Changed |
|-------|-----------|------|-------------|
| Ambulance | SCAD | 97.5% | Young F + prior MI + no NTG relief |
| After ECG/labs | APS vs SCAD | 94.7% / 91.1% | Multi-territory MI + microcytic anemia |

| **After derm exam** | **NF1 Vasculopathy** | **>95%** | ≥6 CAL spots + axillary freckling + neurofibromas |

## Pathophysiology
NF1 gene (17q11.2) → neurofibromin deficiency → unchecked Ras/MAPK signaling → vascular smooth muscle proliferation + intimal hyperplasia → coronary artery aneurysms → turbulent flow → thrombosis → recurrent MI

## Teaching Points

1. **NF1 vasculopathy is underrecognized.** It affects 2-6% of NF1 patients. Most commonly renal arteries (HTN) and cerebral arteries, but coronary involvement — though rare — can be fatal.

2. **Always do a complete skin exam.** The subtle neurofibromas and café-au-lait spots were initially overlooked. It took a formal dermatology consult to identify ≥6 CAL spots, axillary freckling, and neurofibromas that clinched the diagnosis.

3. **The family history was the hidden clue.** Father (SCA at 45) and grandfather (heart disease at 37) likely had NF1 vasculopathy — autosomal dominant across 3 generations. The smoking was an exacerbating factor, not the primary cause.

4. **NF1 vasculopathy is non-inflammatory.** Normal ESR/CRP distinguishes it from vasculitis. The mechanism is neurofibromin-mediated vascular remodeling, not immune-mediated inflammation.

5. **Coronary aneurysms have a narrow differential in young patients**: KD (most common), NF1 vasculopathy, PAN, connective tissue disorders, Behçet. The skin exam was the discriminator.

6. **This case required FIVE diagnostic pivots** — from SCAD → APS → KD → sarcoidosis/NF1 → confirmed NF1. Each new data point fundamentally reshaped the differential. This is why systematic, multi-round clinical reasoning matters.

7. **Bayesian reasoning is only as good as the hypotheses considered.** NF1 was not in our initial Top 10. The probability of the true diagnosis can never exceed the share of probability assigned to "other" until it enters the hypothesis space.
